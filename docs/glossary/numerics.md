# Glossary: Numerics & Solver Mathematics

*Last updated: 2026-07-07. Part of the [Manatee glossary](../glossary.md) — see the index there for all terms and for how entries are structured.*

How the math engine works: MNA, integration, factorization, convergence — explained in systems terms.

### 2f-aliasing / phantom (free) torque
The energy-conservation bug that the counter-torque averaging window exists to prevent: sampling an AC machine's pulsating counter-torque at the slow mechanical cadence would manufacture a spurious, phase-dependent constant torque — free energy from a sampling artifact.

The physics: instantaneous power in a single-phase AC circuit is not constant — it pulsates at *twice* the electrical frequency (p = v·i of two sinusoids at frequency f has a component at 2f). At our ~5 Hz electrical frequency that pulsation is ~10 Hz, which lands right at Vintage Story's ~100 ms mechanical torque re-sum cadence. The systems view: this is classic sampling aliasing — reading a 10 Hz oscillating signal every ~100 ms is sampling at roughly the signal's own frequency, so instead of averaging out, the samples lock onto one phase of the pulsation and read back a steady offset. Depending on that phase, the alternator would appear to demand systematically less (or more) counter-torque than the electrical side actually consumed — the mechanical and electrical energy ledgers would disagree, and one direction of the error is a perpetual-motion machine. The fix (normative per vintage-story.md): the mechanical side never sees an instantaneous sample; it reads the *mean* counter-torque over all electrical substeps since its previous read.

Standard concepts, project phrasing: "2f" pulsating power in single-phase AC machines and *aliasing* from sampling theory are both textbook; the composite name "2f-aliasing" and "phantom/free torque" for the resulting artifact are ours.

**Where it appears:** `docs/vintage-story.md` §2 (Mechanical Network Coupling, "The averaging window is normative").

**References:** aliasing and the sampling theorem: Oppenheim & Schafer, *Discrete-Time Signal Processing*; double-frequency power pulsation in single-phase machines: Fitzgerald, Kingsley & Umans, *Electric Machinery*.

### across-tick current clamp
- A stability rule for adapted Stationeers generators: the current a generator may deliver this tick is limited using information from the *previous* tick's solve, not by switching behavior inside the current solve.

An unconverted ("legacy") Stationeers generator is stamped into the circuit as a voltage source at its tier's nominal voltage, plus a small internal series resistance. Its real constraint — "I can only supply the power I advertised" — is nonlinear, and enforcing it *inside* the solve would mean mode-switching mid-solve (a known source of non-convergence). Instead the limit is applied adaptor-style: each tick, the clamp is computed from the last solved operating point (in `AdaptedSource` it reads the previous solution's effective load resistance and targets the exact EMF, converging in one step for plain resistive loads). `AdaptedSource` itself is **stateless** (api.md §18: `StateSize = 0`, registers no unit) — it carries no per-device ledger. For programmers: it is a feedback controller with a one-tick delay line, deliberately kept outside the equation solver so the solver stays linear and predictable. For EEs: it is a sequential (per-timestep) linearization of a power limit — related in spirit to the load side's across-tick linearization, but a distinct mechanism: a V-source + internal series R whose clamp is set from the previous solution's load resistance, not a G = P/V\_prev² conductance stamp. The one-tick lag is exploitable in principle (oscillating loads); the system-level defense against that energy pump is `AdaptedLoad`'s across-tick energy-debt ledger, which caps consumption on the load side — not a ledger on the source (adversarial review 2026-07-05).
- **Project-coined term**, closest standard concept: successive/sequential linearization of a current (power) limit across timesteps — related in spirit to SPICE source limiting, but applied between solves, not between Newton iterations.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/stationeers.md` (Legacy-Device Adaptor), `docs/design.md` (R18 and the 2026-07-05 doc-review resolutions), `docs/api.md` §18 (`AdaptedSource`); the energy-pump concern in `docs/experiments/2026-07-05-adversarial-playtest.md`.
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (companion models and convergence aids); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (linearized companion models per timestep).

### AdjustEpsilon / ε-gate / ε-no-op
- The solver's built-in threshold below which an `Adjust` (a conductance change on an existing component) is treated as "no change at all," so it costs nothing.

When a device nudges a component's value each tick — the classic case is a constant-power load re-presenting itself as G = P/V² from last tick's voltage — the values stop moving once the circuit settles, but naive code would still pay a matrix refactorization for every microscopic wiggle. The ε-gate absorbs this: an `Adjust` whose new value lies within `AdjustEpsilon` of the stamped one (defined as the conductance ratio G_new/G_old falling inside pinned bounds ≈ exp(∓ε), default ε = 1e-4) degrades to a free tier-0 document write on the cached factorization. In programming terms it is a debounce/memoization layer inside the solver: a converged fleet of Newton-style device loops costs zero refactorizations without any client-side "did it change enough?" logic — and the contract explicitly forbids clients from building their own epsilon gate on top, because the solver's definition is the one the determinism goldens are pinned to. The gate deliberately evaluates no transcendental functions (the bounds are stored bit-exact constants, and custom ε uses a fixed polynomial), so classification is bit-identical across CPU architectures.

**Project-coined term**, closest standard concept: convergence/relative tolerance (SPICE's RELTOL plays the analogous role in deciding when Newton iteration has converged), applied here to incremental matrix-update suppression rather than iteration exit.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §9 (pinned definition, `SolverOptions.AdjustEpsilon`), `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §4 and §9 and sharp-edge #13.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (RELTOL and convergence-tolerance machinery); the ngspice manual (`.options reltol`).

### Analyze (step)
- The one-time symbolic phase of the sparse solver: it studies the *pattern* of the matrix (which entries can be nonzero) and precomputes a fill-reducing ordering and a factorization plan, before any actual numbers are crunched.

Sparse LU factorization creates new nonzeros ("fill-in") whose positions depend only on the matrix's sparsity pattern, not its values. Analyze runs once per pattern: it picks a row/column ordering that minimizes fill and records exactly which memory operations every future numeric factorization will perform. Subsequent Factorize calls then reuse this plan with zero allocation — like compiling a query plan once and executing it many times, versus re-planning on every query. In manatee-core this is the first stage of the `ISolverBackend` Analyze/Factorize/Solve lifecycle; the pivot order is frozen at the first numeric factorization (KLU-style), with a pivot-growth monitor and refactor-from-scratch fallback as hygiene. A topology change (tier 3) forces re-Analyze; conductance changes (tier 2) reuse the symbolic pattern and only refactor numerically; value-only changes (tier 1) never re-Analyze either.

Standard concept: **symbolic analysis / symbolic factorization** in sparse direct solvers (the `analyze` phase in KLU, UMFPACK, CSparse).

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` §Numerics (backend plan); `docs/api.md` §Solver layer (`ISolverBackend` lifecycle, from `experiments/solver-bench/Contract.cs`).

**References:** Davis (2006), *Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems*, SIAM (the CSparse book); Davis & Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, a Direct Sparse Solver for Circuit Simulation Problems", ACM TOMS.

### Back-substitution / forward-back substitution / RHS-only solve (tier-1 cost)
The cheapest step of a linear solve, and the API-facing name for the cheapest solve the engine performs: given an already-computed LU factorization of the unchanged circuit matrix, produce the solution for a new right-hand side by two triangular sweeps, without redoing the factorization.

Solving the MNA system `A·x = b` has two unequal parts: *factorizing* `A` into triangular factors L and U (expensive, roughly cubic in dense terms; near-linear for our sparse matrices) and then *substituting* — forward-solving `L·y = b` then back-solving `U·x = y` (cheap, roughly quadratic dense). Manatee's whole performance model leans on the split: a tier-1 `Drive` call (setting a voltage or current source, or a `SineDrive` setpoint) touches only `b` (source values and Backward-Euler companion history terms), so the solver skips factorization entirely and pays just the triangular sweeps against the cached LU. Linear AC islands exploit the same fact every substep: nothing in `A` changes between substeps, so N substeps cost N back-substitutions, not N factorizations — which is why a linear AC island in steady operation can afford dozens of substeps per game tick, and why `Drive` is documented as safe to call every tick or substep. Programming analogy: factorization is an expensive compiled query plan; back-substitution is executing it with new bind parameters — you only recompile (refactorize) when the schema (conductances/topology) changes. EE analogy: the LU factors are a Thévenin-style pre-reduction of the fixed network, so evaluating a new excitation is trivial. `TickStats.RhsSolves` counts these, with `Refactorizations` expected to be 0 in steady state. Strictly, "back-substitution" names only the upper-triangular half, but here (as commonly in the field) it stands for the whole forward+backward triangular solve.

**Standard term:** forward/back-substitution after LU decomposition (also "backward substitution", the "triangular solve", or "RHS-only solve"); the combined RHS solve against a reused factorization is what SPICE-family simulators do whenever the Jacobian is unchanged. The "tier-1" packaging as a public API contract is project-specific.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R3/R4, Numerics, and Simulation Model; `docs/solver.md` Change-Cost Tiers (tier 1 row); `docs/api.md` §4 (Tier 1: `Drive`, `[T1]`-tagged verbs) and §9 (`TickStats.RhsSolves`); `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §1 verb table and tier table; `docs/testing-strategy.md` benchmarks; tier references in `docs/stationeers.md`.

**References:** Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (triangular systems); Davis, *Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems* (SIAM, 2006 — the KLU lineage); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*.

### Backward Euler
The implicit, first-order numerical integration method Manatee uses to advance energy-storage elements (capacitors, inductors) through time in transient analysis — the project's one and only integrator.

A capacitor's behavior is a differential equation (current depends on the *rate of change* of voltage). To timestep it, each storage element is replaced per step by a "companion model": a resistor plus a source whose values encode the element's state from the previous step (capacitor: G = C/dt with a history current I = G·V_prev; inductor is the mirror image). This turns the differential problem into a plain resistive solve per step — the state-update happens after each solve, like a state machine transitioning on committed output. Backward Euler is "implicit" (the new state is defined in terms of itself, which the matrix solve resolves) and L-stable — the docs call it the "boring but never blows up" choice: it damps rather than amplifies numerical error even at large timesteps, at the cost of first-order accuracy. Manatee makes that trade deliberately: game time constants are chosen at human-visible scales (0.1–10 s) where BE at 50 ms is accurate — the "EA trick". Contrast ngspice, which defaults to trapezoidal/Gear integration; the oracle tests therefore compare at looser tolerance or force matching options (`method=gear maxord=1`) where exactness matters. Trapezoidal is explicitly out of scope until a concrete accuracy need appears.

**Standard term:** backward (implicit) Euler; the companion-model formulation is standard SPICE practice.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Transient), `docs/design.md` (R2, Simulation Model), `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests tolerances), `docs/curriculum.md` (via the EA trick).

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2*, UCB/ERL-M520 (companion models and integration methods); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; the ngspice manual (`.options method=...`).

### Backward Euler (BE)
The implicit numerical integration rule manatee-core uses to advance energy-storage elements (capacitors, inductors) from one timestep to the next.

A capacitor's behavior is a differential equation (current depends on the *rate of change* of voltage); a discrete-time simulator must replace that with an update rule per timestep. Backward Euler evaluates the derivative at the *end* of the step being computed, which makes each step an equation to solve rather than a formula to plug in — that is what "implicit" means, and it is why storage elements become companion models (a conductance G = C/dt plus a history current) stamped into the same linear system as everything else. The payoff is L-stability: no matter how large the timestep relative to the circuit's time constants, the solution decays rather than oscillating or exploding — solver.md calls it the "boring but never blows up" choice. In programming terms, BE trades per-step accuracy for an ironclad invariant (bounded, non-divergent state), like choosing a crash-safe write path over a faster one that can corrupt on a bad schedule. The project deliberately sizes component values so time constants sit at human scales (0.1–10 s), where BE at the 50 ms game tick (or Stationeers' 0.5 s power tick) is accurate anyway — a trick inherited from Electrical Age. Trapezoidal integration (more accurate, less robust) is explicitly out of scope until a concrete need appears. The ngspice oracle tests are pinned to the same rule via `SpiceEmitOptions.MatchBackwardEuler` (`.options method=gear maxord=1` — Gear's method at order 1 *is* Backward Euler), so simulator and oracle integrate identically and comparisons test the stamps, not the integrator choice.

Standard term (also called the implicit Euler method).

**Where it appears:** design.md (R2, Simulation Model), solver.md (Analyses: Transient), testing-strategy.md, api.md §22 (`SpiceEmitOptions.MatchBackwardEuler`).

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (companion models for implicit integration); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (integration rules and stability); the ngspice manual (`method`/`maxord` options).

### Bit-for-bit / EnvFingerprint scope
The project's determinism promise: two runs produce *exactly identical* floating-point bytes — but only when both runs happen inside the same "environment fingerprint" (same .NET runtime, CPU architecture, and build configuration).

Floating-point arithmetic is deterministic on one machine, but the *same source code* can produce slightly different last-digit results on different hardware or runtimes — the JIT compiler may reorder operations or fuse a multiply-and-add into a single FMA instruction that rounds once instead of twice. So Manatee scopes its strongest reproducibility claims (executable law 4: solve → snapshot → restore → step matches a never-snapshotted run bit-for-bit on `Solution.RawVector`) to one `EnvFingerprint` domain. Across domains — CI runs the test corpus on both x64 and arm64 — comparison falls back to numeric tolerances, the same way the ngspice oracle tests compare. For an EE: it is like specifying an instrument's repeatability on one calibrated bench versus reproducibility between labs — different, weaker guarantee. Whether to forbid FMA-contractible patterns in the LU kernel (widening the domain at some speed cost) is explicitly deferred until evidence demands it (api.md §23.5).

**Standard term:** the underlying issue is (non-)reproducibility of IEEE-754 floating point across compilers/architectures; "bit-for-bit" and "bitwise reproducible" are standard phrases. `EnvFingerprint` is the project's name for the equivalence domain.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §14 (executable law 4), §21 (`RawVector` as the bit-for-bit comparison unit), §23.5 (open item on FMA and cross-platform FP).

**References:** Goldberg (1991), "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic", ACM Computing Surveys; IEEE Std 754 (floating-point arithmetic).

### CheckInvariants / MaxKclResidual / AllFinite
A public self-check on a solved island — `island.CheckInvariants(...)` returns an `InvariantReport` — that verifies the solution obeys physics (current balances at every node) and arithmetic sanity (no NaN/Infinity), using the exact same machinery CI uses.

`MaxKclResidual` is the largest violation of Kirchhoff's Current Law across the island's nodes, reported together with `WorstKclNode`: for an EE, it is the summed branch currents at the worst node; for a programmer, it is a conservation invariant — every node is a bank account whose deposits and withdrawals must net to zero, and a nonzero residual answers "which node leaks current." Because MNA solutions satisfy KCL by construction when the stamps are right, a leak indicts a specific component's stamp code, not the linear algebra. `AllFinite` (with `FirstNonFiniteRow`) catches NaN escapes; the release policy is that no NaN ever leaves the API — a non-finite solve faults the island instead, so `AllFinite` failing in a dev build means a bug upstream of that safety net. One code path serves three consumers: CI assertions, the compaction resync backstop, and the tablet's educational "current doesn't balance at this node" messages. The call is `0B` (allocation-free) and safe to assert on in ordinary dev play.

Standard concepts: KCL residual checking is textbook; exposing it as a first-class public readback struct is a project design choice.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §"InvariantReport" (`CheckInvariants`, `MaxKclResidual`, `WorstKclNode`, `AllFinite`, `FirstNonFiniteRow`); `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §8 "Invariants as a debugging tool"; `docs/design.md` Testing; `docs/testing-strategy.md`.

**References:** Ho, C-W., Ruehli, A. & Brennan, P. (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems — KCL as the row equations of MNA; Pillage, L., Rohrer, R. & Visweswariah, C., *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*, McGraw-Hill — residuals and solution checking.

### Conditioning / condition number / conditioning cliff / machine-epsilon conditioning cost
The condition number κ(A) measures how numerically fragile a matrix solve is — how much small input errors get amplified in the answer; the "conditioning cliff" is the point where the spread between the largest and smallest values stamped into the circuit matrix exhausts double precision and solve accuracy collapses; "machine-epsilon conditioning cost" is our benchmark finding that a particular design choice made that fragility no worse than rounding already is.

A double holds ~16 significant decimal digits (machine epsilon ε ≈ 2.2×10⁻¹⁶ is that rounding unit), and as a rule of thumb a solve loses about log₁₀(κ) of them, where κ is roughly proportional to the ratio of the extreme conductances in an island. A well-conditioned matrix loses almost none; an ill-conditioned one (nearly singular — close to describing a circuit with no unique answer) can lose most of them. Our earlier value range (1e9 S closed switch down to 1e-12 S gmin) spanned 21 decades — more range than the digits available, so exactly the pathological builds R9 promises players will make would fall off the cliff. The settled conductance-range policy (see *Conductance-range policy*) keeps stamped conductances in [1e-9, 1e3] S — 12 decades, comfortably below the cliff — and debug builds warn when an island's extreme conductance ratio exceeds 1e12. The "machine-epsilon conditioning cost" claim in the grounding model (design.md): the implicit ~1 MΩ leak resistor stamped from each device negative to earth under `WiringPolicy.TwoWireLeak` was benchmarked to change solve accuracy only at the machine-epsilon level — its effect on conditioning is lost in the noise floor of double precision, so keeping islands grounded this way is numerically free (the *time* cost, 1.5×/2.3× on realistic/ladder circuits, is separate and budgeted). For an EE this is the standard gmin-style leak justification — large enough to pin the null space, small enough not to perturb the solution — and the usual SPICE guidance on avoiding extreme element ratios, made an enforced invariant with a fuzz-test sweep instead of folklore. For a programmer: conditioning is error amplification like a lossy codec whose quality degrades with the dynamic range of the input, and the cliff is an overflow budget — every decade of value spread spends one of a fixed number of digits of headroom.

**Standard terms:** condition number κ(A); "machine epsilon" (also "unit roundoff", ε or u); "conditioning cliff" and "conditioning cost" are our informal names for, respectively, the accuracy collapse when log₁₀(κ) approaches the precision of the arithmetic and the change in a system's achievable residual — the phenomena are textbook.

**Where it appears:** solver.md Numerics (conductance-range policy, "conditioning cliff"); testing-strategy.md Property and Fuzz Tests (conductance-range sweep); design.md Grounding model (two-wire idiom, implicit leak); api.md §5 (`WiringPolicy.TwoWireLeak` comment); docs/experiments/2026-07-05-backend-competition.md (the benchmark).

**References:** Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations*; Higham, *Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms* (SIAM); the ngspice manual (element-value and gmin guidance); IEEE Std 754 (floating-point arithmetic).

### Conductance-range policy
- Manatee's project-wide rule that every conductance stamped into a matrix stays within [1e-9, 1e3] siemens — from a 1 GΩ "open" switch to a 1 mΩ "closed" switch or DC inductor short — with gmin = 1e-12 S.

Circuit simulators never stamp a true zero or a true infinity; "off" and "on" are represented by very small and very large conductances. The policy caps how far apart those extremes may be, because doubles carry ~16 significant digits and an earlier draft's 1e9 S / 1e-12 S spread (21 decades) would hit the conditioning cliff on exactly the pathological player builds requirement R9 promises to survive. The chosen endpoints are SPICE-conventional values. For programmers: it is an input-validation contract on the numeric layer — like clamping user data to a range where your algorithm has proven accuracy bounds, rather than hoping edge cases work. Every component author must respect it; debug builds warn when an island's extreme conductance ratio exceeds 1e12, and a fuzz test axis sweeps values across the full legal range. Settled 2026-07-05.

**Project-coined term**, closest standard concept: dynamic-range limiting of element values for matrix conditioning, plus the standard SPICE GMIN minimum conductance.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Numerics — the settled policy; Analyses — DC inductor shorts), `docs/design.md` (doc-review round 2026-07-05), `docs/testing-strategy.md` (fuzz axis).
- **References:** L. W. Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (GMIN and off-conductance conventions); the ngspice manual (GMIN option).

### Conductance ratio (extreme)
- The ratio between the largest and smallest conductance stamped into a single island's matrix — a cheap early-warning number for how numerically fragile that island's solve is about to become.

Every component contributes conductance entries (siemens) to the island's MNA matrix, and when the biggest and smallest entries are many orders of magnitude apart, the matrix becomes ill-conditioned: double-precision arithmetic carries only ~16 significant digits, so a spread approaching that width means small entries are effectively noise next to large ones and the computed voltages lose accuracy. Manatee's debug builds warn when an island's extreme ratio exceeds 1e12 — which is exactly the worst-case spread the conductance-range policy permits between two legal component conductances (1e3 S vs 1e-9 S = 12 decades), not a point comfortably inside it. A warning therefore means an island has hit the widest conditioning spread the policy allows; ratios beyond that line arise in practice because the numerics also stamp gmin = 1e-12 S shunts (below the component floor) on non-ground diagonals, typically against a large closed-switch conductance. That is still short of the ~16-digit double-precision cliff, but at the policy's own edge. For programmers: this is like monitoring a ratio that predicts catastrophic floating-point cancellation before it happens — a lint-style assertion on numeric health, not a correctness check. The ratio is a fast proxy for the true condition number of the matrix, which is expensive to compute exactly.

**Standard term:** this is a heuristic proxy for the matrix **condition number**; the underlying issue is standard ill-conditioning of the nodal admittance matrix.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Numerics — the conductance-range policy paragraph); exercised by the fuzz axis in `docs/testing-strategy.md`.
- **References:** J. Vlach & K. Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*, ch. on numerical solution of linear equations (conditioning and pivoting); L. Pillage, R. Rohrer & C. Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*.

### Convergence / converged (device settling, tick-loop sense)
- The state where a device's per-tick `Adjust` calls stop meaningfully changing anything — the circuit's voltages have settled, so the solver stops refactorizing and each tick drops to the cheap right-hand-side-only cost.

Devices like the constant-power adaptor re-linearize every tick: they read last tick's voltage, compute a new conductance (G = P/V²), and write it back with `Adjust`. While the voltage is still moving, each write changes the matrix and forces a tier-2 refactorization; once the voltage settles, successive writes land within `AdjustEpsilon` of the value already stamped and degrade to free tier-0 no-ops. For a programmer: it is a fixed-point iteration reaching its fixed point, at which moment writes become idempotent and the cache (the LU factorization) stops being invalidated. For an EE: it is the operating point settling, the same fixed-point idea as Newton iteration but spread across game ticks. Crucially, detection is the *solver's* job — the ε-gate (a pinned, bit-identical ratio comparison, api.md §9) decides whether an Adjust is a no-op; device authors never write their own convergence test. `TickStats.AdjustNoOps` counts converged devices; `Refactorizations > 0` in steady state means some device is *not* converging and is a bug to chase.

Our usage is standard fixed-point-iteration vocabulary, but note the tick-loop twist: the "iteration" runs at one step per game tick across the live simulation, not inside a single solve.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §4 (adaptor loop, ε-no-op) and §9/sharp edges; `docs/api.md` §9 (AdjustEpsilon pinned definition); `TickStats.AdjustNoOps`.
- **References:** Fixed-point iteration and relaxation are covered in Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; the tick-spread linearization is the classic secant/fixed-point treatment of constant-power loads in power-flow literature.

### Convergence / non-convergence (Newton sense)
- Whether the Newton-Raphson iteration used to solve a nonlinear circuit (one containing diodes and similar elements) reaches a stable answer within its iteration budget.

A linear circuit is solved in one matrix solve; a nonlinear one is solved by guessing an operating point, linearizing every nonlinear element around it, solving the resulting linear circuit, and repeating until the answer stops moving — Newton-Raphson iteration. Convergence means that loop terminates at a self-consistent solution. Non-convergence means it oscillates or diverges, which is a normal hazard near sharp nonlinearities like a diode knee. For a programmer: it is a retry loop with no guarantee of termination, so the failure path must be designed, not hoped away. Our contract (design.md R9): non-convergence must produce a *legible* fault — the per-island fallback ladder tries (1) reusing the last operating point, (2) a source-stepping ramp, then (3) marks the island `Faulted` with a named diagnostic (`FaultKind.NewtonDiverged`). Never NaN propagation, never a hang; a Faulted island reads as de-energized. The Newton-robustness fuzz test sweeps randomized diode circuits through the knee to enforce exactly this: converge or Fault legibly.

This is the standard SPICE meaning of "convergence"; source-stepping and gmin are the classic continuation remedies.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R9; `docs/solver.md` Failure Handling (fallback ladder); `docs/testing-strategy.md` Property and Fuzz Tests; `docs/api.md` `FaultKind`, `TickStats.NewtonIterations`.
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (Newton-Raphson and convergence aids); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*; the ngspice manual's convergence chapter (gmin- and source-stepping).

### Coordinate storage / duplicate-summing stamps
- The assembly format for the circuit matrix: it is built as a flat list of (row, column, value) triples, and when several components write to the same cell, their values are added together.

MNA builds the system matrix by "stamping": each component contributes a small fixed pattern of entries (a resistor between nodes i and j adds its conductance at four positions). Rather than updating a finished matrix in place, we record every stamp as a (row, col, value) triple in coordinate (COO) form; converting to the compressed form the factorization needs then *sums duplicates* — all triples landing on the same cell collapse into one entry by addition. This matches the physics exactly (two resistors sharing a node genuinely add their conductances at that cell) and makes assembly trivially incremental and order-independent. For a programmer: it is an append-only event log of matrix contributions, reduced by a group-by-(row,col)-and-sum before use — writers never coordinate, and the reduction step produces the canonical structure. Stamps are additionally versioned so an unchanged (pattern, values, dt) skips assembly entirely and reuses the cached factorization. For an EE: this is the standard element-stamp view of MNA made explicit as a data structure.

**Standard terms:** *coordinate format* (COO, also "triplet form", as in SuiteSparse/CSparse) with *duplicate summation*; "stamp" is standard SPICE/MNA vocabulary.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` Change-Cost Tiers, implementation notes.
- **References:** Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", *IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems* (element stamps); Davis, *Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems*, SIAM (triplet/COO form and duplicate summation in CSparse); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*.

### correctly-rounded / transcendental-free gate
- The rule that the ε-no-op test ("is this resistance change small enough to ignore?") is computed using only floating-point operations that give bit-identical answers on every CPU and runtime.
- IEEE 754 requires basic operations (`+ − × ÷`) to be **correctly rounded** — the result is the representable number nearest the exact answer, so every conforming machine produces the same bits. Transcendental functions (`log`, `exp`) carry no such guarantee: each platform's math library (libm) may differ in the last bit. Manatee's Adjust gate therefore never calls `Math.Log`/`Math.Exp` at classification time. Instead the ε bounds are **pinned constants**: `RatioLo`/`RatioHi` are exact double bit patterns (semantically `exp(∓ε)`) stored at implementation time, and the gate is one IEEE division (`G_new/G_old`) plus two comparisons; custom ε values derive bounds via the polynomial `1 ∓ ε + ε²/2` using only `±`/`×`. The stakes: an Adjust converging near the ε boundary could otherwise classify as a no-op on x64 but trigger a refactorization on arm64, diverging the `Refactorizations`/`BytesAllocated` counters that the dual-architecture tier-budget golden tests assert on. In systems terms, this is removing a platform-dependent input from a function that must be a pure, deterministic classifier; the EE analogy is replacing a comparator whose threshold drifts with temperature by one referenced to a bandgap — the decision must not depend on which unit you bought.
- **Standard term:** "correctly rounded" is standard IEEE 754 vocabulary; the gate design itself is project-specific (a cross-platform-determinism discipline).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §9 (AdjustEpsilon pinned definition) and §24 decision 21.
- **References:** IEEE Std 754-2019, *IEEE Standard for Floating-Point Arithmetic*; Goldberg (1991), "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic", ACM Computing Surveys 23(1); Muller et al., *Handbook of Floating-Point Arithmetic* (Birkhäuser).

### dt
The simulation timestep: the slice of simulated time advanced by each solver step (tablet: 10 ms; Vintage Story electrical tick: 50 ms; Stationeers: 0.5 s, the vanilla power tick).

Each `Step(dt)` advances the circuit by dt seconds; by convention `dt <= 0` requests a DC operating point instead (capacitors open, inductors near-short). dt sets the accuracy of the companion-model integration: energy-storage elements are discretized with Backward Euler, e.g. a capacitor becomes a conductance G = C/dt plus a history current, so a smaller dt tracks fast transients more faithfully at the cost of more solves. Crucially for performance, Manatee keeps dt **fixed** per profile: fixed dt means the BE companion conductances are constants, so the system matrix does not change tick-to-tick and a linear island stays on the cheap tier-1 path (cached factorization, `Drive`-only updates). AC subcycling adapts substep count, and only re-derives dt (a tier-2 event) when the required rate drifts past about ±15%. Programming analogy: dt is the frame interval of a fixed-timestep game loop — fixed so per-frame work is precomputable; changing it invalidates caches.

**Standard term:** yes — timestep / integration step size, written h or Δt in numerical-analysis literature; SPICE calls it the transient step.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (analyses, companion models, subcycled AC); `docs/design.md` (simulation model, per-client dt choices); `docs/vintage-story.md` (Tablet Host, fixed 10 ms dt via accumulator); `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §2 (`NetlistOptions.Stationeers(Dt)`), §5 (tick loop).

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (companion models, transient stepping); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (integration methods and step-size/accuracy tradeoffs).

### dt (timestep)
- The size of the time slice the transient solver advances per step — how much simulated time passes between successive circuit solutions.

In Manatee, dt is deliberately **fixed** per context rather than adaptive: the Vintage Story electrical tick uses dt = 50 ms (with AC islands subdividing that tick into N equal substeps), and Stationeers runs DC operating points plus storage dynamics at dt = 0.5 s (the vanilla power tick). The fixed dt is a load-bearing performance choice, not just a simplification: Backward Euler companion models turn each capacitor into a conductance G = C/dt and each inductor into its mirror, so as long as dt does not change, those conductances are constant — the matrix pattern and values stay valid, and a linear island in steady operation runs entirely on the cheap tier-1 path (reusing the cached LU factorization, like a cache that never invalidates). SPICE-family simulators instead vary dt to control truncation error, paying refactorization on every change; we trade that accuracy knob for predictable per-tick cost. Passing dt ≤ 0 to `Step(dt)` is the sentinel for a DC operating point (capacitors open, inductors near-shorts). For programmers: dt is the frame time of a fixed-timestep game loop; for EEs: it is the integration step h of the companion-model discretization, held constant by design.

Standard term: **timestep / integration step (h)** — standard; our fixed-dt policy (vs. SPICE's adaptive timestep control) is the project-specific part.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Change-Cost Tiers, Analyses; `Step(double dt)` in the netlist API), `docs/design.md` (Simulation Model, perf targets).

**References:** Nagel (1975), SPICE2 memo UCB/ERL-M520 (adaptive timestep control, for contrast); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (companion models and integration step).

### dual convergence test
- The rule for deciding when Newton-Raphson iteration on a nonlinear circuit has actually finished: **both** the solution stopped moving **and** the equations are actually satisfied.

Each Newton iteration produces a new guess for the node voltages. Our stopping rule requires two independent checks to pass simultaneously: a **scaled step-norm criterion** (the change between successive iterates is small, relative to the magnitude of the values — so a microvolt change on a kilovolt node counts as "stopped") and a **residual criterion** (plugging the solution back into the circuit equations leaves only a tiny error — i.e., Kirchhoff's current law nearly balances at every node). Either check alone can lie: a slowly-crawling iteration can take tiny steps while still far from the answer, and a small residual can coexist with an ill-conditioned system whose solution is still shifting. Requiring both is like a distributed-systems health check that demands both "no writes in flight" and "replicas agree" before declaring the system quiesced. If the check never passes within the iteration cap, the failure is surfaced diagnosably rather than silently accepted.

Standard concept — SPICE-family simulators similarly combine a delta (RELTOL/ABSTOL on the update) check with a residual/current check; also called a combined step-and-residual stopping criterion.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Analyses, Nonlinear elements).

**References:** Nagel (1975), SPICE2 memo UCB/ERL-M520 (Newton convergence criteria); the ngspice manual (RELTOL/ABSTOL convergence options); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*.

### relaxation (α) / explicit relaxation / damped fixed-point iteration
Deliberate damping applied to the values two separately-solved islands exchange at a power-transfer boundary each substep, so the back-and-forth iteration between them settles instead of oscillating or blowing up. Called "explicit" in the docs to stress that the damping is a visible, accounted-for design choice — and that the energy it smears is charged to heat, never invented or lost.

When two islands are joined by a boundary (a decoupling transformer, a DC converter two-port), each side solves its own matrix and they trade boundary values — amplitude+phase per AC substep, or power — once per substep. That trade is a fixed-point iteration: each side reacts to the other's last-published (one-substep-stale) value, and feeding it through raw (α = 1) can *ring* when the loop has gain — side A overshoots, side B overcorrects, and the pair diverges. Relaxation blends each newly exchanged value with its previous value: `new = α·raw + (1−α)·old`, the classic exponential smoothing / first-order IIR low-pass / EWMA update, with α tunable per device type. For programmers it is a smoothed moving average on a twitchy control signal, or a learning rate in gradient descent (smaller α = slower but safer convergence); for EEs, a damping resistor added to kill oscillation in a physical loop. The coupled islands are deliberately *not* free-running threads — they are scheduled as one work unit and substep in lockstep — so this is under-relaxation of an iterative fixed-point update carrying a bounded one-substep lag, not a fix for thread asynchrony.

"Explicit" carries two loads. First, the damping is an openly declared numerical device: a transformer's real leakage/magnetizing storage is modeled as honest circuit elements but *not relied on* for stability, because its time constants are shorter than a substep — stability comes from the α-smoothing, on the record and property-tested; DC boundaries get the equivalent storage as a real, honestly-sized DC-link capacitor instead. Second, it feeds the energy-accounting rule: smoothing means one side can momentarily book slightly more or less energy than the other delivered, and that accounting surplus is *debited to device heat* — per-boundary energy ledgers enforce at runtime that no relaxation artifact ever becomes stored work or free energy (a bank-reconciliation invariant: every rounding difference is posted to a named expense account). In Stationeers that heat lands in a real thermal simulation. Stability is enforced, not assumed: for transformer couplers the *effective* α is clamped to ≤ 0.7 — the highest value the exhaustive stability grid (over α × store resistance × turns ratio) verifies dissipative at every corner (gain-capable loops at α ≥ 0.8 produced measured 1e15–1e73 J transients before the clamp), because runaway grows the energy-debt droop's own reference geometrically in lockstep so the debt mechanism cannot substitute for numerical damping; the converter two-port is unclamped because its charge-controller loop is provably contracting. Property tests require that randomized load steps across a boundary settle and never ring unboundedly. (The API parameter that sets α is `RelaxationAlpha`, default 0.5 — see its entry.)

**Standard term:** under-relaxation (relaxation parameter ω < 1), as in Gauss–Seidel/Jacobi iteration and waveform-relaxation co-simulation; "exponential smoothing" is the time-series name for the same update rule (first-order IIR low-pass / EWMA). Successive over-relaxation (ω > 1) is the sibling *acceleration* regime — the opposite of the damping done here. The heat-debit bookkeeping is project-specific.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Islands — coupling devices, exchange scheme); `docs/design.md` (Simulation Model: island coupling; energy-accounting design rule; doc-review round; decision log); `docs/api.md` §7 and §17 (coupler specs — `relaxationAlpha`, the coupler energy ledger, and the α ≤ 0.7 clamp ruling); `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Property and Fuzz Tests: adaptor/boundary-coupling stability).

**References:** Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (relaxation-based solution methods); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (relaxation and partitioned/iterated timing analysis); White & Sangiovanni-Vincentelli, *Relaxation Techniques for the Simulation of VLSI Circuits* (1987).

### fallback ladder
- The ordered sequence of recovery strategies an island's solver tries, one after another, when Newton iteration fails to converge on a nonlinear circuit.

- The rungs, in order: (1) reuse the last known-good operating point as the starting guess and retry; (2) run a *source-stepping* ramp, where all sources are scaled up gradually from zero toward their real values so Newton only ever has to make small corrections; (3) give up and put the island into the `Faulted` state, carrying a diagnostic naming the offending nodes/components. For programmers, this is a classic retry-with-escalation pattern: cheap fix first, expensive fix second, then fail loudly with structured error data rather than returning garbage — no NaN ever leaves the API, and a Faulted island reads as de-energized to the game. For the EE: this is exactly the continuation-method arsenal every SPICE-family simulator keeps behind `.op` failures, just with a fixed, documented ordering. It runs per island, so one player's pathological build cannot fault anyone else's grid.
- **Project-coined term**, closest standard concept: SPICE convergence aids / continuation methods (source stepping, warm-starting from a previous operating point).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md`, Failure Handling section; the Faulted state it terminates in is part of the public API (`docs/api.md`).
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (Newton–Raphson and convergence aids); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (continuation and source-stepping methods); the ngspice manual (convergence helpers for `.op`).

### fill-reducing ordering
- A reshuffling of the matrix's rows and columns, chosen once up front, that keeps the matrix sparse while it is being factorized.

When a sparse matrix is decomposed by LU factorization, elimination creates new nonzero entries ("fill-in") in positions that were zero — like a defragmenter that, run in the wrong order, fragments the disk further. How much fill appears depends dramatically on the order in which rows/columns are eliminated, so the solver's symbolic Analyze step picks a permutation heuristically minimizing it (finding the true optimum is NP-hard; heuristics like approximate minimum degree are used in practice). In manatee-core's in-house KLU-style sparse LU, this ordering and the resulting symbolic plan are computed once per topology change, the pivot order is frozen at the first numeric factorization, and every subsequent tier-2 refactorization reuses the plan allocation-free (a tier-1 change touches only the RHS and re-solves against the existing factors). Circuit matrices reward this: MNA matrices are extremely sparse (a node only couples to its few neighbors), which is why the sparse backend beats dense at every tested size.

Standard term; common concrete algorithms are AMD (approximate minimum degree), COLAMD, and nested dissection.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` Numerics (backend plan); implemented inside the in-house sparse LU's Analyze step.
- **References:** Davis, "Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems" (SIAM, 2006); Amestoy, Davis & Duff (1996), "An Approximate Minimum Degree Ordering Algorithm", SIAM J. Matrix Anal. Appl.; Davis & Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, a Direct Sparse Solver for Circuit Simulation Problems", ACM TOMS.

### Finiteness
- The invariant that no `NaN` or `Inf` ever appears in a solution vector — every voltage and current the solver reports is an actual, finite number.

Floating-point arithmetic has two poison values: `Inf` (overflow, division by zero) and `NaN` ("not a number", e.g. `0/0`), and both are contagious — one bad entry silently corrupts everything computed from it downstream. For an EE: this is the numerical equivalent of a meter that must never read "?" — a pathological circuit may be rejected or reported as a fault, but it must never produce garbage readings that look like data. For a programmer: it is an assertion checked over every solve result, the numerics analogue of "no null pointers escape this API". In Manatee it is one of the core invariant checks in the test suite, and it is the enforcement mechanism behind design requirement R9 (legible failure): degenerate circuits must produce diagnosable errors or a Fault, never NaN propagation into game state.
- This is standard numerical-software practice; the property is usually phrased as values being "finite" in the IEEE 754 sense (`IsFinite` excludes both NaN and the infinities).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Invariant Checks); `docs/design.md` R9; `docs/solver.md` failure handling.
- **References:** IEEE Std 754 (floating-point arithmetic) defines NaN/Inf semantics; Goldberg (1991), "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic", ACM Computing Surveys.

### Fixed sim dt / fixed dt
- A constant simulation timestep — the tablet always advances its circuit in identical 10 ms slices of simulated time, regardless of how fast the game is rendering.

`dt` is the timestep: how much simulated time one solver step covers. The tablet host keeps it fixed and drives it from the game's client tick via an *accumulator*: each frame, real elapsed time is added to a counter, and the sim takes as many whole 10 ms steps as the counter can pay for, carrying the remainder forward. This decouples simulated time from wall-clock time and frame rate — a slow frame means more steps that tick, not a bigger step. Two payoffs: determinism (the same lesson replays bit-identically in-game and in CI, since every run takes the exact same step sequence), and performance (a changed dt would change every companion conductance (G = C/dt), invalidating the cached LU factorization and forcing a per-step tier-2 numeric re-factorization; constant dt keeps the conductances — and thus the factorization — fixed, so only the history source on the right-hand side changes each step, which is the cheap tier-1 fast path). For an EE: a fixed dt means the discrete companion models (a capacitor becomes a fixed conductance plus a history-dependent source) have constant coefficients, so the system matrix and its factorization can be reused across steps.
- Standard terms: "fixed timestep" (the game-loop pattern, often described as "fix your timestep") and "fixed step size" in numerical integration.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/vintage-story.md`, The Tablet Host; change-cost tiers in `docs/solver.md`.
- **References:** the accumulator pattern is canonically described in Glenn Fiedler's "Fix Your Timestep!" (gafferongames.com); fixed-step companion-model reuse is standard SPICE-family practice — see Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, "Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods".

### FMA (fused multiply-add)
- A CPU instruction that computes a×b+c as a single operation with one rounding step instead of two, making the result slightly *more* accurate — and slightly *different* — than doing the multiply and add separately.

Ordinary floating-point arithmetic rounds after every operation; FMA keeps the full-precision product internally and rounds only once after the add. In C/C++, compilers may silently "contract" a written `a*b + c` into an FMA depending on architecture and build flags (`-ffp-contract`), so identical source can produce bit-different results across machines. .NET is stricter: RyuJIT does *not* auto-contract scalar multiply-adds — it emits separate multiply and add to preserve IEEE 754 semantics — so in C# an FMA only enters the picture through an explicit `Math.FusedMultiplyAdd` call or through SIMD/hardware intrinsics (`System.Runtime.Intrinsics`) whose FMA availability differs between x64 and arm64. For an EE, it is like two meters with different internal resolution reading the same signal: both correct within tolerance, but their last digits disagree — a problem only if you demanded identical printouts. Since the LU factorization kernel is a dense chain of multiply-adds, those FMA-capable code paths are the main threat to cross-platform bit-for-bit determinism. Manatee's ruling (api.md §23.5): determinism is scoped to one `EnvFingerprint` (runtime/arch/build); CI runs the corpus on x64 and arm64 with tolerance diffing; whether to forbid FMA-contractible patterns in the LU kernel to widen the bit-exact domain is deferred until evidence demands it.

Standard term, defined in the IEEE 754 floating-point standard (`fusedMultiplyAdd`); in .NET it surfaces as `Math.FusedMultiplyAdd` and the hardware intrinsics API.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §23.5 (cross-platform floating point, open item).

**References:** IEEE Std 754-2019 (fusedMultiplyAdd); Goldberg (1991), "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic", ACM Computing Surveys.

### gmin
A deliberately tiny conductance (10⁻¹² siemens — about a trillion ohms to reference) quietly attached to circuit nodes so the equation system is always mathematically solvable, even when part of the circuit is disconnected from everything.

MNA solves a matrix equation whose unknowns are node voltages; a node with no path to the reference node has *no defined voltage*, which shows up as a singular (unsolvable) matrix — the numeric equivalent of a divide-by-zero. Gmin is the guard value: each island anchors a ground (an explicit ground node if marked, else an arbitrary node) and places gmin shunts on non-ground diagonals, so a floating subgraph gets an answer (~0 V, carrying ~picoamps — far below anything gameplay can observe) instead of crashing the solve. Think of it as a default value that keeps an otherwise-undefined variable from poisoning the computation, chosen small enough to be lossless in practice. It also anchors the conductance-range policy: real stamped conductances live in [10⁻⁹, 10³] S, safely above gmin, so a conductance at or below gmin compares as "effectively zero" in equivalence tests (api.md §9). If a matrix is *still* singular after gmin — genuinely contradictory circuits, which players will build (R9) — the island enters the `Faulted` state with a diagnostic rather than emitting NaNs; gmin *ramping* (gradually relaxing gmin to coax a hard nonlinear solve to converge) is noted in api.md §23.8a as a possible future convergence aid.

This is the standard SPICE term and default value (SPICE's `GMIN` option defaults to 10⁻¹² S); "gmin stepping" is the classic related convergence algorithm.

**Where it appears:** docs/solver.md (Islands, Numerics, Failure Handling); docs/api.md §5 (island construction), §9 (equivalence comparison), §20 (failure table), §23.8a.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; the ngspice manual (`.options GMIN`, gmin stepping); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*.

### GMin / GMax
Clamp bounds on the conductance a device is allowed to stamp into the matrix: GMin is the floor, GMax the ceiling.

A constant-power adaptor computes its conductance each tick as `g = Watts / V²` from last tick's voltage, and that formula explodes as V → 0 (g → ∞, i.e. a dead short). The clamp is the guard rail: whatever the formula says, the stamped value is confined to `[GMin, GMax]` — think of it as input validation on a feedback loop, preventing one bad sample from writing a nonsense value into shared state. GMin is also the *shed* target: a bus that reads genuinely dead or Faulted (below the live floor) gets `g = GMin` rather than letting `P/V²` seek GMax and stamp a fictional short that pops real fuses (sharp-edges appendix, edge 4). Distinguish this per-device clamp (e.g. `gMin: 1e-6, gMax: 1e3` on `AdaptedLoad`) from the solver-wide **gmin** (1e-12 S), the tiny leak conductance shunted from every non-ground node so the matrix stays numerically solvable even when parts of the circuit float — a conditioning trick, not a physical resistor.

**Standard term:** the closest SPICE analogues are **GMIN** (the minimum conductance placed in parallel with pn junctions as a Newton convergence aid) and, for the node-to-ground leak specifically, ngspice's **GSHUNT**/**RSHUNT** options (generically: diagonal loading of the MNA matrix); the project follows solver.md's SPICE-conventional gmin naming. The per-device `[GMin, GMax]` clamp is a project convention (closest standard concept: limiting/clamping in Newton iteration, cf. SPICE's device limiting).

**Where it appears:** `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §4 (AdaptedLoad constructor, the `P/V²` tick body), §6 (live-floor shed), appendix edge 4; solver-wide gmin in `docs/solver.md` (Numerics) and `docs/api.md` §12.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (GMIN and convergence aids); the ngspice manual (`.options GMIN`, `GSHUNT`, `RSHUNT`); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (conditioning and convergence limiting).

### hysteresis
Deliberately using *two different* thresholds for a state change and its reversal, so a value hovering near one threshold cannot flip the state back and forth every tick.

In Manatee it appears in two places. First, the Stationeers adaptor's brownout model (design.md R18): an adapted device *drops off* the network when its voltage falls below `V_low` but only *rejoins* once voltage recovers above a higher `V_high` — plus staggered rejoin delays and a recloser-style lockout — so an overloaded net settles or collapses legibly instead of strobing at tick rate. Second, the AC substep count is "quantized with hysteresis" (see the companion entry). For programmers: it is exactly debouncing a noisy input, or the two-watermark pattern in a cache/queue (evict above the high watermark, refill below the low one) — the gap between the thresholds is a dead band that absorbs jitter. For the EE: this is the standard Schmitt-trigger idea applied to simulation state machines rather than to a comparator.

This is the standard control/electronics term; common aliases are **dead band** and **Schmitt-trigger thresholds**.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R18 (brownout dropout/rejoin) and the doc-review notes; `docs/solver.md` Analyses and `docs/api.md` §11 (`HysteresisBand`) for substep-count quantization; `docs/integration-tutorial.md` (worked brownout example).

**References:** Horowitz & Hill, *The Art of Electronics* (Schmitt trigger / comparator hysteresis).

### initial condition
The stored starting state of an energy-storage element — a capacitor's voltage or an inductor's current — from which a transient simulation begins.

Capacitors and inductors are the stateful objects of a circuit: their differential equations need a starting value before time-stepping can begin, exactly as an integrator loop needs its accumulator initialized. In the Falstad text format each reactive element carries that state inline (`voltdiff` for capacitors, `current` for inductors), with newer files adding explicit `initialVoltage`/`initialCurrent` reset values; Manatee's importer honors these as the element's initial condition, so a circuit saved mid-ring-down resumes ringing rather than starting from zero. For the EE: this is the same notion as SPICE's `IC=` parameter and the `.ic` line / `uic` option — a user-supplied starting state that bypasses or seeds the operating-point computation. For the programmer: it is deserialized element state, the reason save/load of a circuit reproduces its dynamics and not just its topology.

**Where it appears:** `docs/falstad-format.md` §4 (the `c` and `l` element parameter tables) and §7 ("honor `voltdiff`/`initialVoltage` as initial condition; honor initial current").

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (initial conditions and the UIC transient option); the ngspice manual, `.ic` and `IC=` device parameters.

### Integrator
- The numerical rule a transient simulator uses to step components with memory (capacitors, inductors) forward through time.
- A capacitor's current depends on how fast its voltage is *changing*, so simulating it requires approximating a derivative from discrete timesteps. The integrator is that approximation rule — think of it as the update function of a state machine whose state is the previous solution. Manatee uses Backward Euler exclusively (L-stable, the "boring but never blows up" choice; solver.md), realized as companion models: each capacitor/inductor becomes a resistor-plus-source whose values encode the previous step. ngspice defaults to a different integrator (trapezoidal), and two correct integrators legitimately produce slightly different waveforms for the same circuit — different approximations of the same true solution. This is why our oracle tests compare transients at matched timesteps with *looser* tolerances than DC, force ngspice into a matching low-order mode (`.options method=gear maxord=1`-style) where exactness matters, or compare only settled values. Stability is itself exploited as an invariant: both integrators are A-stable, so neither can turn a physically decaying loop into a growing one — a closed motor→alternator loop must not grow, so any growth indicts our coupling code, not the integration scheme. (Backward Euler is additionally L-stable/dissipative; trapezoidal is only marginally stable on lossless modes and can ring, so the invariant is non-growth of the decay envelope, not strict step-by-step monotonic decay.)
- Standard term (aliases: integration method, time-stepping scheme, numerical integration formula in SPICE literature).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` Oracle Tests and Invariants (coupling dissipativity); `docs/solver.md` Transient analysis.
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; the ngspice manual (`.options method`).

### Iteration cap
- The maximum number of Newton-Raphson iterations the solver will attempt on a nonlinear circuit before declaring the solve a failure — a diagnosable one, never a hang or a garbage answer.

Nonlinear elements (diodes, etc.) are solved by repeated linearization: each Newton iteration re-stamps the linearized companion models and re-solves, and the loop exits when a dual convergence test passes (both the scaled step norm and the residual are small). For a programmer, the cap is simply a loop bound on an iterative fixed-point search — the standard defense against a loop whose exit condition may never fire. For the EE, it is the same iteration limit every SPICE-family simulator carries (SPICE's `ITL` parameters). What Manatee emphasizes is what happens on hitting it: instead of aborting the run, the affected island walks a fallback ladder — reuse the last operating point, try a source-stepping ramp, and finally enter a `Faulted` state carrying a diagnostic naming the offending nodes/components. Players build pathological circuits as a hobby (requirement R9), so non-convergence is an expected input, not an exception.
- **Standard term:** Newton iteration limit (SPICE `ITL1`/`ITL4`); ours is standard, with the fallback ladder being the project-specific part.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Nonlinear elements; Failure Handling / fallback ladder).
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (McGraw-Hill, 1995) on Newton-Raphson convergence and source stepping; the ngspice manual (`.options` ITL parameters, GMIN/source stepping).

### Java double / E-notation
- The number format of the Falstad circuit text files Manatee imports: 64-bit floating-point values written the way Java prints them — a decimal point, an optional `E`/`e` exponent, and crucially never a `+` sign in the exponent.

A "double" is the standard 64-bit binary floating-point number (IEEE 754) used by Java, C#, and most languages; "E-notation" is scientific notation in text form, so `1.5E-6` means 1.5 × 10⁻⁶ (for the EE: 1.5 µ — the file format uses plain SI base units with no `10k`/`5u` suffixes). The no-`+` rule is not pedantry: Falstad's file loader tokenizes lines treating `+` as a delimiter (a legacy of circuits pasted from URLs, where a space encodes as `+`), so a number written `1e+5` splits into two tokens and breaks parsing. Java's `Double.toString` never emits `+`, so upstream files are safe by accident; Manatee makes it a rule — parse with `InvariantCulture` (so a French-locale machine doesn't read `,` as the decimal separator) and never emit `+` in exponents when writing. Think of it as a wire-format contract: the accepted grammar is narrower than "any number a human might type."
- **Standard term:** IEEE 754 double-precision / scientific (exponential) notation; the `+`-free constraint is a Falstad-format quirk we honor, and `InvariantCulture` is the .NET name for locale-independent parsing.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/falstad-format.md` §1 (tokenizer), §4 (units convention), §7 (accept/reject contract).
- **References:** IEEE Std 754-2019, *IEEE Standard for Floating-Point Arithmetic*.

### KLU / KLU-style
- KLU is a well-known sparse LU factorization library (part of SuiteSparse, designed specifically for circuit matrices); "KLU-style" in our docs means our in-house solver backend copies its key architectural trick — splitting the expensive one-time *symbolic* analysis from the cheap repeated *numeric* factorization.

Circuit matrices are sparse (mostly zeros), and factoring them has two separable phases: **symbolic** analysis (choose a fill-reducing ordering and plan where nonzeros will land — depends only on the matrix's *pattern*, i.e. the circuit topology) and **numeric** factorization (compute the actual LU values — depends on the numbers). When only component values change (a switch toggles, a Newton iteration updates a diode's conductance — tier 2), the pattern is unchanged, so the symbolic plan is reused and only the cheap numeric pass reruns; this is `refactorize`, and it is the main numeric improvement over the sparky prototype, which refactored from scratch. Programming analogy: symbolic analysis is compiling a query plan; numeric factorization is executing it with new parameters — you don't recompile per execution. Our production backend is an in-house KLU-style sparse LU (fill-reducing ordering + symbolic plan in Analyze, pivot order frozen at first numeric factorization, zero-alloc refactorize/solve); it beat optimized dense LU at every size benchmarked. Linking the actual native KLU library via P/Invoke was **rejected**: shipping per-platform native binaries is a mod-distribution burden.

Standard term: KLU refers to Davis & Palamadai Natarajan's "Clark Kent LU" solver in SuiteSparse; the symbolic/numeric split is standard sparse-direct-solver practice.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` Change-Cost Tiers (tier-2 note) and Numerics (backend decision, revised 2026-07-06 on benchmark evidence; see `docs/experiments/2026-07-05-backend-competition.md`).
- **References:** T. A. Davis & E. Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, A Direct Sparse Solver for Circuit Simulation Problems", ACM Trans. Mathematical Software 37(3); T. A. Davis, *Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems*, SIAM (2006).

### L-stable
- A property of a numerical time-stepping method meaning it not only never blows up on stiff problems at any step size, it actively *damps* fast transients to zero instead of letting them ring.

When a circuit contains time-dependent parts (capacitors, inductors), the solver advances them in discrete time steps, and the choice of integration formula determines what happens to dynamics much faster than the step size. An A-stable method merely guarantees those fast modes don't grow; an L-stable method additionally guarantees they decay toward zero within a step. In programming terms: an L-stable integrator is crash-proof *and* self-quieting — feed it a step size far too coarse for the fastest dynamics in the circuit and it returns a smooth, physically plausible answer rather than a numerically-manufactured oscillation. This is exactly why manatee-core uses Backward Euler (which is L-stable) for all transient analysis — solver.md calls it the "boring but never blows up" choice — and explicitly rejects the more accurate trapezoidal rule (A-stable but *not* L-stable, so it can ring artificially at coarse game-tick step sizes) until a concrete accuracy need appears. Since a game cannot choose its step size freely (the tick rate is fixed), robustness at arbitrary step size beats accuracy.

This is the standard numerical-analysis term (due to Ehle); common context terms are *A-stable* (the weaker guarantee) and *stiff decay*.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Analyses — Transient: Backward Euler companion models).

**References:** Hairer & Wanner, *Solving Ordinary Differential Equations II: Stiff and Differential-Algebraic Problems*, Springer; Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL memo M520 (integration-method trade-offs in circuit simulation); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*, McGraw-Hill.

### Last operating point / last-good read
- The most recent successfully-converged solution for an island, kept around and reused: as the starting guess when the next nonlinear solve begins, and as the value readers see while the island cannot currently produce a fresh answer.

This one saved vector serves two distinct jobs. **As a Newton seed** ("last operating point", the numerics job): nonlinear solves use Newton-Raphson, an iterative guess-and-refine loop, and starting from last tick's answer rather than from zero usually converges in a step or two because circuits change little between game ticks — the same idea as a warm cache. It is also rung 1 of the per-island fallback ladder when Newton fails to converge: retry from the last operating point, then source-stepping, then declare the island `Faulted`. **As a read fallback** ("last-good read", the API job): while an island is mid-rebuild or awaiting a solve, readers get the last *published* values instead of garbage or manufactured zeros; a `Faulted` island is the exception and reads as de-energized, though the pre-fault vector is still held internally to warm-start the retry. In EE terms, the seed role is exactly SPICE's standard practice of continuing a transient from the previous timepoint's operating point.

Standard aliases for the seed role: *previous operating point*, *warm start*, *initial guess reuse*. The read-fallback role is project-defined API semantics (see the separate "last-good read" entry).

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Failure Handling — fallback ladder); `docs/api.md` §24 fault table ("previous solution held internally (retry warm-start)").

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL memo M520 (Newton-Raphson with previous-timepoint initial guesses; source stepping); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*, Van Nostrand Reinhold.

### Linearization (across ticks)
- Manatee's technique for handling a nonlinear constant-power device cheaply: pretend it is an ordinary resistor whose value is computed from *last* tick's voltage, and let it converge over several game ticks instead of iterating within one solve.

A constant-power load (I = P/V) is nonlinear, and solving it properly needs Newton iteration inside the tick. R18's adaptor sidesteps that: each tick it stamps the device as a plain conductance G = P/V_prev² (clamped), using the voltage from the previous tick. The value is stale by one tick, so the answer is only approximately right at any instant, but in normal operation it converges to the true operating point over a few ticks — the game's tick loop *is* the fixed-point (successive-substitution) iteration, spread through time. The stale value creates an exploit surface: a load that oscillates could harvest the lag as free energy, so each adapted device carries an across-tick **energy ledger** (deliverable this tick = advertised − accumulated debt) that makes the error bounded and unpumpable. Programming analogy: it's a cached derived value used one frame late, paired with an accounting invariant that guarantees the cache-staleness error can never accumulate in the user's favor.

**Standard term:** closest standard concept is a fixed-point / successive-substitution iteration on the operating point (as opposed to per-solve Newton–Raphson), with the iteration amortized over simulation ticks. The energy ledger is project-specific.
- **Where it appears:** design.md R18; stationeers.md "The Legacy-Device Adaptor"; integration-tutorial.md (per-tick body of the constant-power adaptor).
- **References:** Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods* (nonlinear device iteration and companion models).

### LTE (local truncation error)
- The small error a time-stepping integrator commits on each individual step, because it approximates a smooth curve with a straight-line (or low-order) segment.

Manatee integrates storage elements (capacitors and inductors) with Backward Euler at a *fixed* step size, so LTE is controlled by design-time choices rather than by a runtime step controller: component values are chosen so time constants land at human-visible scales (0.1–10 s), where a 50 ms step tracks the true exponential accurately, and AC islands subcycle at roughly 20 samples per cycle of the highest source frequency so each step covers only a small slice of the sinusoid. (Batteries are not on this path: they are behavioral device-layer models — EMF plus internal resistance plus a state-of-charge integrator — not BE-integrated storage elements.) For a programmer: this is like choosing a polling interval much shorter than the fastest thing you need to observe — instead of an adaptive rate limiter, the system guarantees accuracy by construction. The docs never name the term explicitly, but it is the quantity those "~20 samples/cycle" and "0.1–10 s time constants" targets exist to bound; ngspice oracle tests, not analytic error bounds, are what certify the result. Standard aliases: local error, one-step error (as opposed to *global* error, the accumulated drift over many steps).

**Where it appears:** implicit in design.md Simulation Model (DC/Transient/AC step-size rationale) and solver.md Analyses; accuracy is enforced by the oracle suites in testing-strategy.md.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (SPICE's LTE-based timestep control — the adaptive alternative Manatee deliberately avoids); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (truncation error of Backward Euler and other integration formulas).

### LU factorization / cached LU / Factorize
Decomposing an island's MNA matrix into a **L**ower- and an **U**pper-triangular factor (A = LU) so the system can be solved cheaply by substitution, and keeping those factors in memory ("cached LU") so repeated solves with the same matrix pay only the cheap final step.

Solving the MNA system `Ax = b` splits into an expensive one-time factorization of `A` into L and U, then a cheap forward/back substitution against `b` (see *Back-substitution*). If nothing about the circuit's conductances or topology changes between ticks, `A` is bit-identical, the factorization is reused, and each substep costs only a substitution — exactly a memoization cache whose key is effectively the (sparsity pattern, conductance values, dt) triple: a tier-1 change touches only `b` and hits the cache, a tier-2 conductance change invalidates only the numeric layer (numeric refactorization, symbolic pattern reused), and a tier-3 topology change rebuilds everything. This is the load-bearing fact behind requirement R3's subcycled AC: with fixed dt the Backward-Euler companion conductances are constant, so a linear AC island runs many substeps per game tick against one cached factorization. For a programmer, factorization is an expensive compiled query plan and a substep is a cheap lookup-plus-apply; "cached LU" in the docs is shorthand for "we are on the fast path where the factors are still valid." Manatee's production backend is an in-house KLU-style *sparse* LU (see *sparse LU / dense LU / in-house sparse LU*), chosen on 2026-07-06 benchmark evidence over dense and CSparse.NET; a naive dense LU survives only as a CI referee.

In the solver-backend API this surfaces through the internal `ISolverBackend` Analyze → Factorize → Solve lifecycle: `Analyze` does the one-time symbolic work (sparsity pattern, fill-reducing ordering), `Factorize` produces the numeric factors, and `Solve` applies them by substitution. Two calls to `Factorize` are not alike, and the test suite depends on the difference: the *first* `Factorize` allocates the symbolic pattern and chooses pivots; the *second and later* calls land on the frozen-refactor path — pivot order frozen, zero heap allocations. This is classic warm-up-then-steady-state, like a JIT compiling on first call, which is why the zero-allocation gates in testing-strategy.md must warm past the first `Factorize` before measuring, or they would measure setup cost. "Frozen pivots" is safe here because stamped conductances are confined to [1e-9, 1e3] S and the choice was stress-tested across that range, with a pivot-growth monitor and refactor-from-scratch fallback as cheap hygiene.

**Standard term:** LU factorization (the caching/reuse of factors across solves is standard simulator practice, usually described as "reusing the factorization"; for the pattern-only half, "symbolic reuse"); the frozen-pivot refactorize is what KLU calls `refactor`.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R3/R4, System Overview (per-tick cost — "linear islands: cached LU"), and the Open Questions/decision log; `docs/solver.md` (Change-Cost Tiers — tier 1; Numerics — backend decision); `docs/api.md` §Solver (`ISolverBackend` Analyze/Factorize/Solve), §21 allocation buckets (LU workspace); `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Benchmarks and zero-alloc gate warm-up rules).

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (LU with reuse in the transient loop); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Davis & Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, A Direct Sparse Solver for Circuit Simulation Problems", *ACM TOMS* (the analyze/factor/refactor lifecycle this API mirrors); Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (LU generally); Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", *IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems* (the MNA system being factored).

### matrix change
Any edit that forces the solver to rebuild or re-stamp its system matrix — the expensive kind of change, as opposed to updating side data that the matrix never sees.

MNA solves a circuit by assembling one large system of linear equations (the conductance matrix) and factorizing it; changing a resistance value or the wiring topology invalidates that factorization and costs real solve time. Manatee's docs use "matrix change" as the cost boundary to design around: for example, when the ambient temperature changes on Europa vs. Vulcan, the compaction layer's limit envelopes (melt/ampacity thresholds) are recomputed as a **metadata-only recompute — never a matrix change** — because thresholds are checked against solver outputs rather than stamped into the equations. For programmers: think of the matrix as a compiled artifact and its factorization as a build cache — a matrix change is a cache-invalidating edit, while metadata changes are like editing a comment: no rebuild. For EEs: the distinction is between changing the network itself and changing only what you compare its readings against.

This usage is standard in circuit-simulation practice; related terms are "re-stamping" and "refactorization" of the MNA matrix.

**Where it appears:** `docs/compaction.md` Responsibilities #4 (limit attribution); the change-cost tiers in `docs/solver.md`.

**References:** Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*.

### maxTimeStep
The field in a Falstad circuit file's `$` header line that gives the transient-simulation timestep in seconds — the only header field Manatee treats as electrically meaningful.

Falstad/circuitjs text files begin with a `$ flags maxTimeStep simSpeed currentSpeed voltageRange ...` header; everything else on that line controls how the circuit is *displayed* (animation speed, plot scaling), while `maxTimeStep` sets how finely time is sliced when integrating capacitors and inductors. Manatee's importer validates the header's arity and numerics, consumes `maxTimeStep` as its transient-dt hint, and ignores the display fields. For programmers: dt is the tick length of the simulation loop — smaller ticks mean more accurate integration of time-dependent state at higher CPU cost, exactly like a game physics step. Despite the "max" in the name, Manatee uses it as *the* step hint rather than an adaptive upper bound.

**Standard term:** transient timestep (dt / TSTEP in SPICE `.tran`); "maxTimeStep" is circuitjs's field name, where an adaptive stepper may go below it.

**Where it appears:** `docs/falstad-format.md` §2 (header spec) and §7 (importer accept-list: "consume `maxTimeStep` as the transient-dt hint; ignore the display fields").

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL memo M520 (the `.tran TSTEP` lineage); the ngspice manual, transient analysis chapter.

### `method=gear maxord=1`
- An ngspice option pair that forces its transient integrator down to first-order Gear, which is mathematically the same method as our Backward Euler — used so oracle tests can demand exact timestep-by-timestep agreement.

By default ngspice integrates transients with trapezoidal (or higher-order Gear) methods, while manatee-core uses Backward Euler; both are legitimate, but they produce slightly different waveforms between settled states, so a naive diff would fail for reasons that aren't bugs. Setting `.options method=gear maxord=1` caps ngspice's Gear (BDF) family at order 1, which *is* Backward Euler, removing the method mismatch. Systems analogy: it's pinning a dependency to the exact same algorithm version on both sides of a differential test, so any remaining disagreement is a real defect rather than version skew. Our testing strategy uses this only "where exactness matters"; other transient oracles use looser tolerances or compare settled (steady-state) values only.

Standard SPICE options; Gear's method is also known as BDF (backward differentiation formulas), and BDF1 = Backward Euler.

**Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests — transient tolerance rules).
- **References:** Gear, C.W. (1971) *Numerical Initial Value Problems in Ordinary Differential Equations*, Prentice-Hall; Nagel (1975) SPICE2 memo UCB/ERL-M520; the ngspice manual (`.options method`, `maxord`).

### Metric prefixes
- The SI multiplier prefixes — milli- (×10⁻³), kilo- (×10³), micro-, mega-, and so on — as they appear in the tablet's instrument readouts, taught as a curriculum appendix.

Circuit quantities span enormous ranges (microamps to kiloamps, millivolts to kilovolts), so meters display values with prefixes rather than long decimal strings: "4.7 kΩ", "12 mA". The appendix exists because the game's target audience includes players who have never internalized that 1 mA = 0.001 A, and misreading a prefix by a factor of a thousand is exactly the kind of error that makes readouts useless. For programmers, a prefix is a floating scale factor — a named power-of-1000 exponent applied to the displayed mantissa — and it belongs purely to the human-facing display layer: the solver itself always works in plain SI base units (volts, amps, ohms) and prefixes are pure formatting. Teaching prefixes via readouts follows Electrical Age precedent. This is entirely standard usage.

### minTimeStep
- An optional trailing field in the Falstad circuit-file header line that records the smallest timestep the simulator's adaptive time-stepping is allowed to take.
- In a transient (time-domain) simulation with adaptive stepping, the solver shrinks its timestep when the waveform changes fast and grows it when things are quiet; `minTimeStep` is the floor on that shrinking. In the Falstad `$`-header it is the last of two optional fields (after `powerBarValue`), and the reference reader simply absorbs its absence with a try/catch (`CircuitLoader.java:262-265`) — older files without it still parse. For our importer it is a pass-through display/settings value, not something manatee-core acts on; `maxTimeStep` is the header field with actual electrical meaning. Programming analogy: an optional config key with a lenient parser — missing means "use defaults", present means "clamp here".
- This is standard adaptive-timestep vocabulary; SPICE-family simulators expose the same idea as a minimum internal timestep (ngspice's transient analysis has an analogous floor below which it aborts with "timestep too small").
- **Where it appears:** `docs/falstad-format.md` §2 (header line grammar and the canonical `lrc.txt` example).
- **References:** ngspice manual, transient analysis chapter (adaptive timestep control); Falstad/circuitjs1 source `CircuitLoader.java`.

### monotonic decay
- The required shape of a passive closed loop's stored energy over time: it only ever goes down (or stays flat), never up — the standing test that the coupled mechanical–electrical simulation creates no energy from nothing.

Manatee co-simulates Vintage Story's mechanical shaft network (~100 ms updates) with a faster electrical simulation, exchanging torque and load between them. Because single-phase AC power pulsates at twice the electrical frequency, a *sampled* counter-torque could alias into a phase-dependent DC offset — phantom (or free) torque, i.e. a perpetual-motion bug. The fix is normative averaging: the alternator reports the mean counter-torque over all electrical substeps since the last mechanical read. The invariant test that proves the fix: a closed motor→shaft→alternator→motor loop with no source must **monotonically decay from any initial condition**. In programming terms, it's a liveness/safety property checked over a trace — energy is a variant function that must be non-increasing at every observation, so a single uptick anywhere is a test failure, with no tolerance-band judgment calls needed.

The underlying idea is standard: passive systems dissipate energy (a Lyapunov-function argument); "phantom torque from sampling" is an aliasing artifact in co-simulation.

**Where it appears:** `docs/vintage-story.md` §2 (the normative averaging window for `GetResistance()`); `docs/testing-strategy.md` (standing invariant test).

**References:** aliasing from undersampling is the Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem (any signals text, e.g. Oppenheim & Willsky, *Signals and Systems*); energy-based stability arguments are Lyapunov's direct method (e.g. Khalil, *Nonlinear Systems*).

### NaN / Inf
- Special floating-point values meaning "not a number" (the result of an undefined operation like 0/0) and "infinity" (the result of overflow or division by zero); Manatee treats both as malformed input and rejects them loudly.

IEEE 754, the universal standard for computer arithmetic on real numbers, reserves bit patterns for these non-finite values so that bad math produces a marker instead of a crash. The danger is that the marker is contagious — almost any arithmetic involving a NaN yields another NaN — so one slip silently corrupts everything downstream, like a single open connection pulling an entire measurement chain to a meaningless reading. Manatee's Falstad importer therefore rejects any NaN or Inf in a circuit file at parse time, with a line number and reason, the same way it rejects non-positive resistances: garbage is refused at the door, never simulated. For an EE: think of NaN as a "reading invalid" flag on a meter that, once raised, taints every calculation that touches it.

Standard terminology from IEEE 754 (aliases: "non-finite values"; Inf is also written "infinity", and NaN comes in quiet/signaling flavors, a distinction Manatee does not rely on).

**Where it appears:** `docs/falstad-format.md` §7 (importer reject list); `docs/testing-strategy.md` (invariants).

**References:** IEEE Std 754-2019, *IEEE Standard for Floating-Point Arithmetic*; David Goldberg, "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic", ACM Computing Surveys 23(1), 1991.

### Newton-Raphson / Newton iteration
The repeat-until-converged loop the solver uses to handle components whose behavior is not a straight line (diodes and similar): guess an operating point, replace each nonlinear device with the straight-line (resistor + source) companion that matches its curve at that guess, solve the now-linear circuit, and repeat from the improved guess until successive answers stop moving.

The core MNA machinery can only solve *linear* systems, so a diode (current exponential in voltage) is handled by iteration. For a programmer it is a bounded fixed-point loop — `guess → linearize → solve → new guess` — with a convergence test as the exit condition and an iteration cap as the watchdog. Each iteration re-stamps every nonlinear device's linearized companion, so its conductance changes and every iteration is a **tier-2 event** (numeric refactorization, symbolic pattern reused). Convergence is judged by a **dual test** — the scaled step norm (the answer stopped moving) AND the residual (the equations are actually satisfied, i.e. KCL balances) — and diode voltage updates are limited per SPICE practice to stop the exponential overflowing. Manatee's design rule (the "nonlinearity budget") is that Newton exists because the tablet curriculum teaches diodes, but it is kept out of the per-substep hot path of world AC islands: devices that would need it there (converters, power supplies) ship as behavioral two-port models instead, so hot islands stay linear and pay only cheap tier-1 costs.

When the loop hits its iteration cap without settling — a normal hazard near sharp nonlinearities like a diode knee — the solver does not give up immediately but climbs a per-island **fallback ladder** (the "Newton ladder"): (1) restart from the last known-good operating point, (2) source-stepping (ramp the sources up from zero so each step starts near an already-solved circuit), then (3) mark the island `Faulted` with a diagnostic, recording `FaultKind.NewtonDiverged`. "Newton ladder exhausted" is one of the two documented triggers of the `Ready → Dirty → Faulted` island state-machine transition (the other is a singular matrix), and a Faulted island reads as de-energized until a later edit lets it retry; no NaN ever escapes, never a hang. This contract is enforced by the **Newton-robustness** property/fuzz tests: randomized, seeded diode circuits are swept through the knee, asserting per R9 ("players build pathological circuits as a hobby") that every solve either converges to a valid answer or Faults legibly — the invariant under test is the absence of the two illegal terminal behaviors (a NaN escaping the API, or non-termination), not the numeric answer itself.

**Standard term:** Newton–Raphson iteration (the universal nonlinear solve in SPICE-family simulators; the two names are used interchangeably in our docs). "Newton ladder" is a project coinage; source-stepping and gmin-stepping are the classic continuation remedies. The robustness sweep is property-based/fuzz testing.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` (Simulation Model, "Nonlinearity budget"; R9); `docs/solver.md` (Analyses — Nonlinear elements; Change-Cost Tiers, tier 2 "per-Newton-iteration"; Failure Handling); `docs/api.md` §9 (`TickStats.NewtonIterations`), §11 (`FaultKind.NewtonDiverged`, `IslandStatus` state machine), §17 fault table, §20; `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Property and Fuzz Tests — "Newton robustness").

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (Newton–Raphson and convergence aids); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*; the ngspice manual (source-stepping and gmin-stepping options); Claessen & Hughes (2000), "QuickCheck: A Lightweight Tool for Random Testing of Haskell Programs", ICFP (property-based testing).

### Non-convergence
- Failure of the iterative (Newton-Raphson) solve to settle on an answer within the iteration cap — one of the fault conditions with a defined, legible fallback rather than a crash or NaN.

Nonlinear elements make the circuit equations unsolvable in one shot, so the solver iterates: guess a solution, linearize around it, re-solve, repeat until successive guesses stop moving (a dual test on scaled step norm AND residual). If the iterates never settle — oscillating between states or wandering off — that's non-convergence. Programming analogy: a fixed-point loop with a retry limit; non-convergence is hitting the limit without the exit condition ever becoming true. Per requirement R9 ("legible failure"), our response is a fallback ladder, per island: (1) reuse the last operating point, (2) source-stepping ramp, (3) mark the island `Faulted` with a diagnostic. In the Stationeers integration, a faulted network additionally drops to Re-Volt's existing scalar power distribution for the tick, so the game never stalls on the solver. No NaN ever leaves the API.

This is the standard numerical-analysis term; SPICE literature also says "convergence failure".

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R9; `docs/solver.md` — Failure Handling (fallback ladder); `docs/stationeers.md` — Failure and Fallback.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (Newton-Raphson convergence in circuit simulation); Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*; the ngspice manual (convergence-aid options such as source stepping).

### numeric refactorization / reused symbolic
Recomputing only the *numbers* of the cached LU factorization when conductance values in the matrix change, while reusing the previously computed structural plan (the symbolic pattern) unchanged — the middle rung, **tier 2**, of the solver's change-cost ladder (requirement R4).

Sparse LU factorization splits into two phases: a **symbolic** phase that depends only on *which* matrix entries are nonzero (it picks orderings and predicts fill-in — expensive, allocation-heavy) and a **numeric** phase that fills in the actual values (cheap, zero-allocation in our backend with the pivot order frozen at the first factorization). When a switch toggles, a variable resistor moves, a fuse blows, or a Newton iteration re-stamps a device, the sparsity pattern is unchanged — only values moved — so Manatee redoes just the numeric phase. R4 requires the API to make change costs *legible* in exactly three tiers, and this labels the middle one, sitting between tier 1 (values on the right-hand side only: reuse the LU entirely, just substitute) and tier 3 (topology change: redo symbolic and numeric from scratch). Every mutator in the public API is doc-tagged with its tier, so a client can predict cost from the call: toggling a switch triggers numeric refactorization; changing a source's setpoint does not. Physically, tier 2 is the circuit's resistive structure changing while its wiring diagram stays the same. Programming analogy: the symbolic pattern is a compiled query plan; numeric refactorization re-executes it with new parameters instead of re-planning. EE analogy: re-running a measurement with the same test-jig — the fixture (symbolic plan) is already built, only the readings (numeric values) are redone.

This symbolic/numeric split is Manatee's main numeric improvement over the earlier sparky prototype, which refactored from scratch every time, and it is what makes tier-2 cheap enough for per-Newton-iteration use; the pivot order is frozen from the first factorization (pivot-growth monitor with refactor-from-scratch fallback as hygiene). Each such re-run increments `TickStats.Refactorizations`, and the binding steady-state contract is that a converged circuit performs **zero** of them — the `AdjustEpsilon` gate classifies value changes landing close enough to the stamped value as no-ops that stay on the cached factorization. testing-strategy.md benches this path directly (the standing "switch-toggle refactor" suite exercises tier 2, and a CI allocation gate asserts tiers 1–2 allocate zero bytes after warmup).

**Standard term:** numeric refactorization with reused symbolic analysis — the design of KLU and other SuiteSparse solvers (`refactor` vs `factor`; also UMFPACK, SuperLU); solver.md calls it "KLU-style refactor".

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R4; `docs/solver.md` "Change-Cost Tiers" (tier-2 row and implementation notes) and "Numerics" (symbolic/numeric split as the main improvement over sparky); `docs/api.md` §4 (`Adjust`) and §9 (`TickStats.Refactorizations`); `docs/testing-strategy.md` standing suites (switch-toggle refactor bench, `TierBudgetGateTests`, allocation assertion).

### .op / DC operating point
A solve for the circuit's steady state with all time-dependence removed: the set of node voltages and branch currents the circuit settles into when nothing is changing.

Mathematically, capacitors are treated as open circuits and inductors as short circuits, and the remaining (possibly nonlinear) algebraic system is solved once. For a programmer: it is the fixed point of the circuit — like asking a converging iterative system for its final state directly instead of stepping it through time. `.op` is the SPICE/ngspice card that requests this analysis; Manatee's docs use ".op" and "DC operating point" interchangeably. It matters here twice: it is one of the three analyses required by design.md R2 (DC operating point, transient, subcycled AC), and it is the oracle mechanism for DC correctness — a test-only translator emits SPICE decks from manatee netlists, ngspice runs `.op`, and results are diffed against manatee's within 0.1% relative (or 1 µV absolute near zero).

Standard term: DC operating point analysis, also called bias-point or quiescent-point analysis; `.op` is standard SPICE syntax.

**Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests); `docs/design.md` R2.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, memo UCB/ERL-M520; the ngspice manual (`.op` analysis); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*.

### Pareto-minimal set / Pareto i²t envelope
- The way a collapsed chain of conductors remembers its fuse-like thermal limits: as a set of (current rating, melt energy, cooling time constant) triples where no triple is worse than another on all three axes, instead of one blended "worst of each" pair.

When the reduction layer collapses a series run of cable segments into one equivalent resistor, each segment's i²t protection (how much accumulated current-squared-times-time it tolerates before melting — the physics behind a fuse) must survive the collapse. The naive move — take the minimum rating from segment X and the minimum melt threshold from segment Y and store that single hybrid pair — fabricates a component that never existed, and it can trip when *no real segment would have*: a false positive invented by the data reduction. Instead we keep every `(rating, meltI2t, tau)` triple that is not **dominated**, and accumulate i²t against each survivor. Dominance is a three-coordinate test: triple *d* retires triple *s* only when `rating_d ≤ rating_s` AND `melt_d ≤ melt_s` AND *d* cools no faster than *s* (the tau axis). The cooling constant participates because a slower-cooling segment can out-trip a nominally weaker one under pulsed load — it ratchets heat up between pulses that a fast-cooling fuse sheds — so being worse in rating and melt is not enough to be discarded. The engine's special case `tau ≤ 0` means "never cools" and ranks as the *slowest* cooling (+∞), and a tau≤0 regression test pins exactly the failure where ranking it as the numeric minimum let a cooling fuse wrongly retire a never-cooling segment. Because every segment in a series chain carries the *same* current, per-triple accumulation is exactly equivalent to simulating the raw, uncollapsed segments — the compaction stays lossless for limit behavior. Programmers can read this as the classic Pareto-frontier pruning used in multi-objective optimization, applied as a cache-compression trick: discard entries provably shadowed by stricter ones, keep the frontier. Ruled 2026-07-06.

**Project-coined term**, closest standard concept: **Pareto frontier / non-dominated set** (multi-objective optimization) applied to fuse i²t (Joule-integral) let-through ratings.

**Where it appears:** `docs/compaction.md` (status header and Responsibilities #4, "Limit attribution"); as-built contract in `docs/api.md` §19 (which spells out the three-axis dominance rule and the tau≤0 semantics); implemented in `core/Manatee.Core/Reduction/ConductorGraph*`.

### phasor analysis
- Solving an AC circuit with complex numbers so each sinusoidal quantity is represented by a single amplitude-and-phase value instead of being stepped through time — in Manatee this is a possible *later* metering optimization, never the primary AC mode.

In standard AC theory, if every source is a sine wave at one frequency and the circuit is linear, you can skip simulating the waveform entirely: replace each signal with a complex number (a "phasor") encoding its amplitude and phase, replace L and C with complex impedances, and solve one complex linear system. For a programmer: it is a closed-form shortcut that computes the steady-state answer directly, like replacing a simulation loop with an analytic formula — valid only when the steady-state assumption holds. Manatee deliberately inverts the usual priority (design.md R3, "waveform-first AC"): AC islands are simulated by subcycled time-domain steps, because real waveforms are the educational point (5 Hz lamp flicker, the oscilloscope item) and time-domain handles non-sinusoidal and transient behavior uniformly. A complex-valued solve path is reserved room in the stamp interface (solver.md Numerics) but stays deferred until cheap steady-state metering is actually wanted; "frequency-domain-only AC" is an explicit non-goal. The curriculum even teaches impedance and phase "without the word phasor" (lesson 15).
- This is the standard EE term (also called *frequency-domain* or *AC small-signal analysis*; SPICE's `.AC` analysis is the canonical implementation). What is project-specific is only its demotion to an optional optimization.
- **Where it appears:** design.md R3 and Non-Goals; solver.md Analyses and Numerics (deferred complex solve path); curriculum.md lesson 15.
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (AC analysis); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Horowitz & Hill, *The Art of Electronics* (phasor treatment of reactance).

### piecewise-constant per tick
- The rule that a slowly-refreshed quantity (shaft speed, and through it AC frequency and source EMF) is treated as one fixed number for the whole duration of each faster electrical tick.

Vintage Story's mechanical power network is server-authoritative and re-sums torque only every ~100 ms, while the electrical simulation ticks (and subcycles) much faster. Rather than interpolating or guessing intermediate speeds, Manatee freezes the last-known shaft speed across each electrical tick and lets it step when the mechanical side next publishes (vintage-story.md §2, "Cadence consequence"). Programmers can read this as two loosely coupled services exchanging snapshots at different rates rather than sharing live state; EEs will recognize a multirate co-simulation with a zero-order hold at the interface. The return path is symmetric and deliberate: the alternator's counter-torque is reported back *time-averaged* over the window, not sampled — a normative rule, because single-phase power pulsates at twice electrical frequency and a sampled torque would alias into a phantom DC torque. Loose coupling is stable here because both simulations have inertia (rotating mass on one side, L/C energy storage on the other).
- Standard concept; the literature terms are **zero-order hold** at a **multirate co-simulation** interface.
- **Where it appears:** vintage-story.md §2 (Cadence consequence, averaging-window rule); design.md Simulation Model; solver.md Analyses.

### piecewise-constant source parameter
- The specific contract by which shaft speed enters the electrical solver: as a parameter of the AC source that is held constant per tick and overwritten at each ~100 ms mechanical re-sum.

This is the mechanical→electrical half of Manatee's co-simulation seam (design.md, Simulation Model). The mechanical network computes shaft speed on its own cadence; the client hands it to the solver's sine-source driver, where it fixes frequency and EMF amplitude until the next update. Crucially it is a *parameter change*, not a topology or conductance change: updating it is a tier-1 (RHS-only) operation, the driver stays phase-continuous across the step so the waveform never glitches, and the substep count N is quantized with hysteresis so ordinary speed jitter never forces a refactorization. A programmer can think of it as a config value hot-reloaded into a running loop through a well-defined setter, deliberately decoupling two systems that tick at different rates; an EE can think of a prime mover whose speed is sampled and held while the electrical network responds continuously. The reverse coupling (counter-torque, time-averaged) completes the loop.
- Standard concept (zero-order-hold input in a loosely coupled multirate co-simulation); the phrase itself is just our doc wording, not a coinage with special semantics.
- **Where it appears:** design.md Simulation Model (mechanical co-simulation); solver.md Analyses (source driver); api.md `Drive(SineDrive)` / the `SineDrive` struct (§22.b, api.md:1753 frequency-input note); docs/experiments/2026-07-05-vs-mechanics.md.

### Pivot / pivoting / frozen pivot order
The pivot is the matrix entry LU factorization divides by at each elimination step; *pivoting* is reordering rows/columns so that entry is safely large (numerical stability); a *frozen* pivot order means Manatee chooses that order once, at the first numeric factorization, and reuses it for every subsequent solve instead of re-choosing it each time.

If the pivot is tiny relative to the rest of its row/column, rounding error in floating-point doubles gets amplified and the computed solution drifts — the numerical analogue of a lossy step you cannot undo. Classical pivoting swaps in a better-placed entry before dividing; in sparse solvers the pivot choice also trades stability against "fill-in" (new nonzeros that make the factorization bigger and slower). Rather than pivoting dynamically on every factorization (safer but slower, and it forces re-allocation of the sparse data structures), Manatee's in-house KLU-style sparse LU computes a fill-reducing ordering and symbolic plan in its `Analyze` phase, freezes the pivot order at the first numeric factorization, and then runs zero-allocation refactorize/solve on the hot path — analogous to compiling a query plan once and re-executing it, trusting the data shape does not change enough to invalidate it. The solver leans on gmin and a bounded conductance range ([1e-9, 1e3] S) to keep the matrix well-conditioned instead of dynamic pivoting; that deliberately narrow range is what makes freezing safe. The gamble was stress-tested across the full legal conductance range (200 full redraws, worst scaled residual 1.7e-9, zero failures), and a cheap pivot-growth monitor with a refactor-from-scratch fallback guards the residual risk (see the *Pivot-growth monitor* and *refactor-from-scratch fallback* entries). This lives in `Manatee.Core.Solver` as part of the sparse-LU gmin/pivot logic.

**Standard term:** pivoting (partial/full/Markowitz pivoting in the sparse-LU literature) — textbook numerical linear algebra; static pivoting / pivot-order reuse in refactorization is how KLU (SuiteSparse) and SPICE-family simulators handle repeated solves of a fixed-sparsity-pattern matrix ("analyze once, refactor many").

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` § Numerics (backend plan); `docs/api.md` §2 (`Manatee.Core.Solver`, "gmin/pivot logic"); `docs/experiments/2026-07-05-backend-competition.md`; realized in the in-house sparse LU backend behind `ISolverBackend`.

**References:** Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations*; Duff, Erisman & Reid, *Direct Methods for Sparse Matrices*; Davis & Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, a direct sparse solver for circuit simulation problems", ACM TOMS; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (Markowitz-style pivoting in circuit simulators).

### Pivot-growth monitor
A cheap runtime check that watches the LU factorization for signs of numerical instability (element growth) and, if tripped, throws away the frozen pivot order and refactorizes from scratch.

Because Manatee freezes the pivot order after the first numeric factorization (for speed), there is a theoretical risk that later circuit states make that order a bad one — pivots shrinking while other entries grow, amplifying rounding error. The classical symptom is "element growth": entries in the factors becoming much larger than entries in the original matrix. The monitor tracks this ratio during refactorization; if it exceeds a threshold, the solver falls back to a full analyze/re-pivot pass. It is the numerical equivalent of a checksum with a rebuild-the-index fallback: not part of the correctness argument, just a tripwire. The docs are explicit that this is "cheap hygiene, not a stability requirement" — stress testing across the full legal conductance range showed the frozen order is robust on its own (worst scaled residual 1.7e-9, zero failures).

**Standard term:** growth-factor monitoring (the growth factor is a standard stability diagnostic for Gaussian elimination); the fallback pattern matches sparse solvers that re-pivot when static pivoting degrades.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` § Numerics (backend plan bullet).

**References:** Higham, *Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms* (growth factor in Gaussian elimination); Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations*.

### refactor-from-scratch fallback
- The solver's safety net for its frozen pivot order: if a numeric health check (the pivot-growth monitor) reports that the reused ordering has gone bad, the factorization is redone from scratch, including choosing a fresh pivot order.
- Manatee's sparse LU backend picks its pivot order once — at the first numeric factorization — and freezes it, so later tier-2 refactorizations can reuse the same plan at zero allocation. That reuse is a bet: the frozen order was chosen for the *old* numbers, and after enough conductance change it could in principle become numerically poor (large "pivot growth", meaning small divisors amplify rounding error). The pivot-growth monitor watches for that, and this fallback is what fires when it trips: throw away the frozen plan and refactor as if for the first time. In programming terms it is a cache with a validity check — serve from the cached plan on the fast path, invalidate and rebuild on a detected staleness signal. Stress testing across the full legal conductance range (200 full redraws, worst scaled residual 1.7e-9, zero failures) showed the fallback is essentially never needed; the docs classify it as "cheap hygiene, not a stability requirement". This is the same discipline production solvers like KLU use (refactor with frozen pivots, full factorization on trouble).
- **Standard term:** this is the standard recovery for partial-pivoting-free refactorization; in the sparse-solver literature it appears as "repivoting" or falling back from `refactor` to a full `factor` (e.g. KLU's `klu_refactor` vs `klu_factor`, guarded by a pivot-growth estimate).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Numerics — backend plan, frozen-pivot robustness).
- **References:** T. A. Davis & E. Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, a direct sparse solver for circuit simulation problems", ACM TOMS; Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (pivot growth and LU stability).

### Refactorizations
- A per-tick counter in `TickStats` recording how many tier-2 numeric refactorizations the solver performed; its load-bearing property is that a converged circuit reads **0**.
- Every `Netlist.Solve` publishes `LastTickStats`, a struct of cheap counters designed to be logged on every tick, and `Refactorizations` is the tier-2 line item. In a healthy steady state, all device `Adjust` calls land within `AdjustEpsilon` of their already-stamped conductances and are absorbed as no-ops on the cached factorization (counted separately in `AdjustNoOps`), so this counter stays at zero. A persistently nonzero value during quiet operation is a diagnosis, not noise: some device's per-tick math is not converging — oscillating or drifting conductance updates that keep punching through the ε-gate — and the tutorial's advice is to check the device's math before suspecting the solver. Think of it as a cache-miss counter whose SLO is zero: CI enforces `Assert.Equal(0, stats.Refactorizations)` on the standing steady-state test, making the performance model executable rather than aspirational.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §9 (`TickStats`, tier-budget CI assertions); `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §9 (the performance contract).

### RelaxationAlpha
The API parameter (on `DecouplingTransformer` and `ConverterTwoPort` coupler specs, default 0.5) that sets the damping factor α for a coupler's per-substep boundary exchange.

It accepts any value in (0, 1], but for transformer couplers the *effective* damping is `min(α, 0.7)`: 0.7 is the proven numerical stability bound for gain-capable loops, established by an exhaustive stability grid over α × store resistance × turns ratio, so configuring 0.9 silently behaves as 0.7 — don't tune past the clamp expecting faster settling (sharp-edge #9 in the tutorial). The clamp exists because runaway in a gain-capable loop grows the energy-debt droop's own reference geometrically in lockstep, so the debt mechanism cannot substitute for numerical damping. Converter two-ports take the configured α unclamped, since their charge-controller loop is provably contracting. Think of it as a config knob whose value is validated against a hard safety envelope: the setting is accepted, the envelope wins.

**Project-coined term** (an API parameter name), closest standard concept: relaxation / damping factor in under-relaxed fixed-point iteration.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §17 (`CouplerSpec.DecouplingTransformer`, `CouplerSpec.ConverterTwoPort`, and the clamp ruling), `docs/integration-tutorial.md` sharp-edges appendix item 9, `docs/design.md` (Islands).

### residual
- The leftover error after a solve: how far the computed solution is from actually satisfying the circuit equations, ideally zero.

For a linear solve of `Ax = b`, the residual is `b − Ax` evaluated with the computed `x`; in circuit terms each entry is the net current imbalance at a node, so "residual ≈ 0" is Kirchhoff's Current Law holding numerically. For a programmer: it is a checksum you can recompute after the fact to see whether the answer is self-consistent, independent of how it was obtained. We use the residual in three roles: (1) as a **convergence test** in Newton–Raphson — an iteration is accepted only when both the scaled step norm *and* the residual are small (solver.md, nonlinear elements); (2) as an **always-on invariant** — KCL residual ≈ 0 is asserted at every node after every solve in debug builds (design.md, testing); (3) as a **stress metric** for the frozen-pivot LU backend, whose robustness test reported a worst *scaled* residual of 1.7e-9 over 200 full redraws (scaled means normalized against the sizes of `A`, `x`, `b`, so it is comparable across problems). Standard numerical-analysis term, used in the standard sense; the two-condition convergence test follows SPICE practice.

**Where it appears:** docs/solver.md (Analyses — dual convergence test; Numerics — frozen-pivot stress result); docs/design.md (Testing — invariant checks).

**References:** Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations*, on residuals and backward error; Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520, for SPICE convergence criteria; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*.

### RHS (right-hand side) / RHS-only value change
The vector **b** in the linear system **Ax = b** that the solver builds each step — it holds the circuit's excitations (source values and companion-model history terms) while the matrix **A** holds its conductances — and, as an adjective, any edit or solver step that touches only **b** and is therefore answered by substitution against the cached factorization alone.

Every solve in Manatee reduces to "factor A once, then solve Ax = b": A encodes how components are connected and how conductive they are; b encodes what is *driving* the circuit right now — voltage/current source values plus the Backward-Euler history terms that make capacitors and inductors remember their state. The two sides change at very different rates: A changes only when conductance or topology changes, but b changes every tick and every AC substep. Since the expensive LU factorization belongs entirely to A, a "RHS-only value change" (the tier-1 edit class — e.g. `SetSourceValue` on a voltage or current source, a generator's output drifting, a battery sagging, the sine source advancing phase) reuses the cached factorization and costs just one forward/back substitution. "RHS-only" is used as an adjective on both edits ("RHS-only value changes", design.md R4) and steps ("RHS-only substeps", design.md R3): in each case A and its LU are untouched, so the cost is O(nonzeros) instead of a refactorization. This is what makes subcycled AC affordable — with fixed dt the Backward-Euler companion conductances are constant, so a linear island in steady operation lives entirely in RHS-only work, tick after tick. Contrast tier 2 (conductance change: numeric refactorization with reused symbolic pattern) and tier 3 (topology change: full rebuild). For the EE: the network is identical, only the drive changed, so the same solved network answers the new stimulus. For the programmer: same compiled query plan, new bind parameters.

**Standard term:** right-hand-side update / solving with a cached (reused) LU factorization; "RHS" is universal numerical-linear-algebra vocabulary (also "source vector" or "excitation vector" in MNA literature). The "tier-1" packaging as a public API contract is project-specific.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R3/R4 and the Simulation Model (linear islands live in tier 1 during steady operation); `docs/solver.md` (Change-Cost Tiers table, Analyses); `docs/api.md` §4 (tier notation `[T1]`-tagged verbs), §15, §21; `docs/testing-strategy.md` benchmarks (the ladder-DC standing suite pins the tier-1 path).

**References:** Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (LU factorization and back-substitution); Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*.

### samples per cycle
- The time-resolution target that decides how many small solver steps an AC island takes per game tick: at least ~20 solver samples for every full swing of its fastest sine wave.

When an island (an independently solved sub-circuit) contains sinusoidal sources, stepping it once per 50 ms game tick would badly undersample the waveform — like polling a fast-changing sensor too slowly and reconstructing garbage. So Manatee subcycles: the island runs N substeps per tick, where N is derived from the island's highest source frequency so that each sinusoid cycle is sampled at least 20 times (e.g. 5 Hz → 100 Hz substep rate → 5 substeps per 50 ms tick). For a programmer, it is a sampling-rate floor, kin to the Nyquist criterion but far above the bare minimum because we want accurate waveform *shapes*, not just detectability. N is quantized with hysteresis so the substep size dt stays constant under small mechanical-speed jitter (a dt change would force recomputing every Backward-Euler companion conductance, a tier-2 event). In the API this surfaces as `SolverProfile.Mixed(tickDt, acSamplesPerCycle = 20)`.

This is the standard notion of "points per period" / time-step-per-period used to set transient step size in circuit simulators; our specific ~20 floor and per-island hysteretic quantization are project policy.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` (Simulation Model), `docs/solver.md` (Analyses, Subcycled AC), `docs/api.md` §on `SolverProfile.Mixed`.
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (transient time-step control relative to signal period); the ngspice manual (transient analysis, `tstep` selection); Oppenheim & Schafer, *Discrete-Time Signal Processing* (sampling theory background).

### scaled step norm
- A measure of how big the latest Newton-Raphson correction was, with each entry scaled relative to the size of the value it corrects; it must fall below tolerance for the nonlinear solve to be declared converged.

When the solver iterates on a nonlinear circuit, each Newton iteration produces an update vector Δx (the proposed change to every node voltage and branch current). Raw size is meaningless across mixed magnitudes — a 0.001 change is negligible on a 230 V node but enormous on a 1 mV sense line — so each component is scaled by a tolerance of the form `reltol·|x| + abstol` before taking the norm, exactly as SPICE does. Manatee uses this as one half of a **dual convergence test**: the scaled step norm must be small (the answer stopped moving) *and* the residual must be small (the answer actually satisfies the circuit equations, i.e. KCL). Requiring both closes the classic false-convergence hole where iterates creep slowly while still being far from a solution. For programmers: it is a fixpoint check with per-field relative epsilons, paired with an independent invariant check on the result.
- **Standard term:** standard Newton-Raphson convergence criterion; SPICE literature calls the per-variable form the update/delta check governed by `RELTOL`/`VNTOL`/`ABSTOL`.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Analyses — Nonlinear elements).
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*; the ngspice manual (options `RELTOL`, `ABSTOL`, `VNTOL`).

### settled values
- The values a transient (time-stepped) simulation converges to after the start-up wiggles die out — the steady state — used as the comparison target when tick-by-tick matching against the oracle would be unfair.
- Manatee's transient integrator (Backward Euler) and ngspice's default integrator take legitimately different paths through time: each timestep's numbers differ slightly even when both are correct, because integration error differs per method. So the oracle tests either force ngspice into a matching low-order mode (`.options method=gear` with max order 1) or ignore the trajectory entirely and diff only the settled values within tolerance. For a programmer, this is like comparing two implementations of an eventually-consistent system by their fixed point rather than their intermediate states: the invariant is "same destination", not "same route". An EE would recognize it as comparing the DC steady state after transients decay, rather than the transient waveform itself.
- **Standard concept**; common aliases: steady-state values, final values (as in the final-value theorem).
- **Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests, transient tolerance bullet); also echoed in convergence invariants ("must settle, never ring").
- **References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (transient integration methods); the ngspice manual (`.tran`, `.options method`).

### singular (matrix)
- A matrix that cannot be solved for a unique answer — the circuit as described does not pin down one set of node voltages.

Each solve is "find the vector x such that Ax = b", where A encodes the circuit's connections and conductances. A is *singular* when that equation has no solution or infinitely many — typically because part of the circuit is electrically floating (nothing ties its voltage to anything, so any offset works) or because two ideal sources contradict each other (no voltage satisfies both). For a programmer: it is the linear-algebra equivalent of an under- or over-constrained system — like a config file that either omits a required field or sets it twice with different values. Manatee's remedies (gmin shunts on floating subgraphs, the Newton fallback ladder) prevent most singularities; if a solve is still singular after all remedies, the island is marked `Faulted` with a diagnostic naming the offending nodes/components, per design requirement R9 ("players build pathological circuits as a hobby"). No NaN ever leaves the API.
- This is the standard linear-algebra term; aliases: *degenerate*, *non-invertible*, *rank-deficient* matrix.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Failure Handling, Islands), `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §8, `docs/design.md` R9; surfaced as `FaultKind.Singular` in `docs/api.md`.
- **References:** Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (formulation and solvability of MNA systems); Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (singularity and rank).

### singular / nonsingular matrix
- The solvable/unsolvable split for the linear system behind each solve: a *nonsingular* matrix yields exactly one solution; a *singular* one yields none or infinitely many.

The solver's job each tick is Ax = b, and everything depends on A being nonsingular — that is the invariant the API works to maintain. Two mechanisms defend it: the wiring policy requires exactly one reference node (MNA datum) per island, and gmin shunts (tiny 1e-12 S leak conductances stamped on non-ground diagonals, see §5) keep fully floating subgraphs nonsingular by weakly tying every node to the reference. Think of gmin as a default value that keeps an otherwise-unset variable from being undefined: it changes the answer by a negligible amount but guarantees there *is* an answer. If a solve comes back singular even after gmin, the island transitions to `Faulted` with `FaultKind.Singular` (§11); the error model (§20) guarantees the previous solution is held and no non-finite value escapes.
- Standard linear-algebra terms; nonsingular is also called *invertible* or *full-rank*.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §5 (wiring policy / gmin), §11 (`FaultKind`), §20 (error model); `docs/solver.md` Numerics and Failure Handling.

### singularity
- The condition of a circuit whose equations have no unique solution; in the Stationeers integration it names the specific hazard of wiring two disagreeing ideal voltage sources directly in parallel.

An ideal voltage source *insists* its terminals sit at exactly its set voltage. Put two in parallel with different set voltages and the math is asked to make one pair of nodes be two voltages at once — a contradiction with no answer, which shows up as a singular matrix. In systems terms it is two writers claiming exclusive ownership of the same variable with different values. The docs call this a "designed-in singularity" because the legacy-device adaptor could easily create it: vanilla Stationeers generators are adapted as voltage sources, and players routinely parallel generators. The fix is structural: every adapted source carries a small internal series resistance, so paralleled sources become a well-posed load-sharing problem instead of a contradiction. If an island nonetheless faults (singularity or non-convergence), the fallback ladder takes over: that network reverts to Re-Volt's scalar ratio distribution for the tick and logs a diagnostic — the game never stalls on the solver.
- Standard usage: a singular system of circuit equations. (Contradictory parallel sources are the textbook example of an ill-posed network; real sources always have internal resistance, which is exactly what the adaptor adds.)
- **Where it appears:** `docs/stationeers.md` (Legacy-Device Adaptor; Failure and Fallback), pointing back to `docs/solver.md` Failure Handling; reported as `FaultKind.Singular` / `ContradictorySources` in `docs/api.md` §11.
- **References:** Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (ill-posed source configurations); Horowitz & Hill, *The Art of Electronics* (source internal resistance / Thévenin view).

### solution vector
- The array of unknowns a single solve computes: every node's voltage, followed by the extra branch currents that MNA introduces for voltage sources and similar components.

An MNA solve is "solve **Ax = b**": **A** encodes the circuit's connectivity and component values, **b** encodes the sources, and **x** — the solution vector — is the answer. Its layout (which slot means which node or branch) is fixed when the island's structure is compiled and does not change between solves, so slot *i* always means the same physical quantity until the next rebuild — like a struct layout frozen at compile time. Everything a client reads (`Voltage`, `Current`, probes) is ultimately a lookup or small computation over this vector; `RawVector` exposes it directly. Our testing strategy treats it as a first-class invariant surface: no NaN or infinity may ever appear anywhere in a solution vector (finiteness invariant), and golden/replay tests compare solution vectors bit-for-bit.

This is the standard term in circuit-simulation literature (also "solution of the MNA system", or **x** in Ax = b).

- **Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Invariant Checks: finiteness); `docs/solver.md` (failure handling); `docs/api.md` §10 (`RawVector`) and the fault table ("non-finite in a solution vector").
- **References:** Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*.

### source-stepping ramp
A convergence aid that, when the nonlinear solve fails outright, retries by ramping all source values up from zero toward their real targets in stages, solving at each stage.

Circuits with nonlinear components (diodes, etc.) are solved by Newton–Raphson iteration, which needs a reasonable starting guess. When it diverges, the solver replays the problem as a sequence of easier ones: at 0% source strength every answer is trivially zero volts everywhere; each small increase in source strength is then solved starting from the previous stage's answer, which is close by construction. Programmers can think of it as bisection-flavored incremental computation: instead of one hard jump to the target state, take many small steps where each step's solution seeds the next. In Manatee it is rung 2 of the per-island fallback ladder for Newton non-convergence: (1) reuse the last operating point, (2) source-stepping ramp, (3) mark the island `Faulted` with a diagnostic. This is the standard SPICE technique of the same name (a *continuation* or *homotopy* method); a sibling technique, gmin-stepping, ramps a stabilizing conductance instead.

**Where it appears:** `docs/solver.md` (Failure Handling — the fallback ladder).

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2*, UCB/ERL-M520; Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (continuation methods for DC convergence); the ngspice manual (`.options` for source-stepping in OP analysis).

### sparse LU / dense LU / in-house sparse LU
Two implementations of the same matrix-factorization step at the heart of the solver: the *sparse* version exploits the fact that most circuit-matrix entries are zero and is Manatee's sole production backend; the *dense* version stores and processes every entry and is kept only as a CI cross-check referee (it no longer runs in any game).

Solving a circuit by MNA ends in factorizing a matrix, and circuit matrices are naturally *sparse* — each node couples only to its few neighbors, so a 100-node island's matrix has a handful of nonzeros per row, not 100. A dense solver stores and processes every entry anyway (O(n²) memory, O(n³) work); a sparse solver keeps only the nonzeros in a compressed structure and skips the zero arithmetic — for programmers, the difference between an adjacency-matrix and an adjacency-list view of a graph. Manatee's in-house backend is a KLU-style sparse LU: a fill-reducing ordering and symbolic plan are computed once in `Analyze`, the pivot order is frozen at the first numeric factorization, and subsequent refactorize/solve calls are zero-allocation — like compiling a query plan once and re-executing it cheaply as values change. Once factored, each solve is a cheap forward/back substitution, which is what makes tier-1 `Drive` calls fast (cached LU reused, only the right-hand side changes). The 2026-07-05/06 backend competition revised the earlier dense-primary plan: in-house sparse LU beat optimized dense at *every* island size tested — n=100: 907 ns vs 2.5 µs — and strictly dominated CSparse.NET, so it is the primary and only production backend (it meets the zero-allocation CI gate at every island size). The naive dense LU survives purely as a **test referee**: CI solves the same circuits with both and requires cross-agreement, so a bug would have to appear identically in two very different implementations to slip through — the numerical analogue of differential testing. CSparse.NET remains a dev-time equivalence oracle only, never shipped (which also keeps LGPL out of distribution). Both backends live in the internal `Manatee.Core.Solver` namespace. Frozen-pivot robustness was stress-tested across the full legal conductance range; a pivot-growth monitor with refactor-from-scratch fallback is kept as cheap hygiene.

**Standard terms:** "dense" vs "sparse" direct solvers (aliases: sparse Gaussian elimination, direct sparse solve); the symbolic/numeric split and frozen (static) pivoting follow the design of SuiteSparse's KLU, a solver built specifically for circuit matrices. Our only twists are the referee-only role of the dense backend and "referee" as project color.

**Where it appears:** `docs/api.md` §2 (`Manatee.Core.Solver` — "in-house sparse LU (sole production backend), dense LU (test referee)") and §Solver namespace (`ISolverBackend`, Analyze/Factorize/Solve lifecycle) and §perf gates; `docs/solver.md` (Numerics — backend plan, incl. the symbolic/numeric factorization split); `docs/design.md` (decision revision, doc-review round); `docs/testing-strategy.md` (cross-agreement in CI); `docs/experiments/2026-07-05-backend-competition.md`.

### stamp / stampCurrentSource (circuitjs cross-reference)
- The falstad-format spec's citation of circuitjs1's own assembly routine, `stampCurrentSource`, used to infer which direction a current source pushes current — a sign convention we deliberately refuse to trust without a test.
- When importing Falstad/circuitjs text files, Manatee must reproduce the original simulator's polarity conventions. `docs/falstad-format.md` pins the current-source direction by reading circuitjs1's source: `stampCurrentSource(nodes[0], nodes[1], currentValue)` at `CurrentElm.java:107-110` implies a positive `currentValue` drives current from post 1 to post 2 *through* the source (arrow toward post 2). Stamping a current source is the simplest stamp there is — it only touches the right-hand-side vector, adding −I at one node's equation and +I at the other's — which is exactly why the sign is easy to get backwards silently. Per the project's working agreement that "the math is untrusted input," the doc flags this convention (and voltage-source polarity, post 2 = +) as the two facts to pin with an ngspice/circuitjs golden test rather than trusting documentation, including our own. For a programmer: this is verifying a third-party wire format's byte order against a reference implementation instead of its README.
- **Standard term:** "stamping" is standard SPICE vocabulary; `stampCurrentSource` is a circuitjs1 (Falstad simulator) method name, cited as source-of-truth evidence.

### substep / subcycling / subcycled AC
The numerical scheme behind Manatee's AC mode: run N small Backward-Euler time steps (substeps) inside each game tick so sinusoidal waveforms are resolved point-by-point rather than averaged away.

Per requirement R3 ("waveform-first AC"), AC is *not* solved with phasors (the complex-number frequency-domain shortcut) but by honest time-domain integration: each AC island subcycles N steps of size `dt = tickDt/N` per tick, N chosen from the island's highest source frequency at about 20 samples per cycle (a 50 ms VS tick is far too coarse for a 5–50 Hz waveform). This is the project's *primary* AC mode, with phasor analysis relegated to a possible later metering optimization. The numerics that make it affordable: the substep size is held constant (N changes only past a ±15% hysteresis band), so every Backward-Euler companion conductance stays fixed, the LU factorization stays cached, and each substep costs only a right-hand-side rebuild plus forward/back substitution — O(nonzeros), tier 1, not a refactorization. It is the classic cache pattern: keep the expensive precomputed object (the factorization) valid by refusing to perturb the parameters it depends on. For a programmer, subcycling is an inner loop at a higher sample rate nested inside the outer game loop, exactly analogous to a physics engine taking several fixed integration steps per rendered frame. The DC profile (Stationeers) never subcycles.

**Standard term:** fixed-step sub-stepping / oversampling within an outer timestep. True *multirate* integration (different subsystems at different step sizes) is what happens *across* islands, each picking its own N; within one island this is plain transient analysis at a fine timestep. "Subcycled AC" as an analysis-mode name is project-coined; a SPICE user would call it plain transient analysis run at a fine timestep, distinguished from small-signal AC (phasor) analysis.

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R3 and Simulation Model ("AC (VS)"); `docs/solver.md` Analyses ("Subcycled AC (VS)"); `docs/integration-tutorial.md` §2; `docs/api.md` §11 (`SubstepPlan`); `docs/testing-strategy.md` property tests and benchmarks; referenced via *substep* in `docs/vintage-story.md`.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520 (transient analysis with companion models); Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", *IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems*; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*; Gear & Wells (1984), "Multirate linear multistep methods", *BIT Numerical Mathematics* 24 (the across-islands multirate case); Golub & Van Loan, *Matrix Computations* (reuse of an LU factorization via back-substitution).

### Substep / electrical substep / substep count
One of the N small solver steps an AC island takes inside a single game tick — the finest time granularity at which the electrical simulation produces values — and the *substep count* N chosen per island that sets how many there are.

Each substep advances the island by `SubstepDt = tickDt/N` using the cached factorization (tier-1 cost when linear), re-evaluating sources at their new phase via the phase-continuous source driver and updating Backward-Euler storage state. N is decided per island from its highest source frequency at ≥ 20 samples per cycle — e.g. a 5 Hz waterwheel generator in a 50 ms tick needs a 100 Hz sample rate, so N = 5; a 50 Hz machine needs ~50 substeps. Crucially N is *quantized with hysteresis*: it changes only when the required rate drifts past roughly ±15% of what N was last chosen for, because changing N changes dt and therefore every capacitor/inductor companion conductance — a deliberate, rare tier-2 (numeric refactorization) event rather than a per-tick one. Think of it as debouncing a configuration value: the input (mechanical frequency) jitters continuously, but the derived setting (N) only flips when the drift is real.

The substep matters beyond the solver in two ways. It defines the **averaging window** for co-simulation with Vintage Story's mechanical network: single-phase AC power pulsates at twice the electrical frequency, so if the mechanical code *sampled* the alternator's counter-torque at one instant it would alias into a phantom phase-dependent DC torque. The normative rule (settled 2026-07-06) is that the reported counter-torque is the **mean over all electrical substeps since the previous mechanical read** — a classic anti-aliasing fix, the same reason audio pipelines average or filter before downsampling — and that read must be a pure lookup of the cached mean, valid at any time on the server main thread. The substep is also the evaluation granularity for **limits** (fuse i²t integrals sum per substep) and for `WaveformTap` sampling.

**Standard term:** inner (minor) time step of a transient analysis; the ±15% hysteresis rule is project policy, and the substep-averaged torque exchange is standard loose (co-simulation) coupling with anti-aliasing.

**Where it appears:** `docs/vintage-story.md` §2 (averaging window, Mechanical Network Coupling); `docs/design.md` R3, Simulation Model, and Mechanical co-simulation; `docs/solver.md` "Subcycled AC (VS)" and Open Questions; `docs/api.md` §11 (`SubstepPlan.SubstepDt`, `SubstepPlan.Substeps`), §12 (per-substep limit evaluation), `TickStats.Substeps`.

### symbolic factorization / symbolic analysis
The reusable, structure-only half of solving a sparse circuit matrix: a one-time analysis of *where* the nonzero entries are — producing an elimination ordering and a plan of where fill-in will land — kept and reused while only the numeric values change. In solver.md's usage it is the `Analyze` step of the in-house sparse LU backend.

Solving the MNA equations means LU-factorizing a sparse matrix, and that work splits cleanly: a **symbolic** part depends only on the sparsity pattern (which positions are nonzero — i.e. which nodes connect to which), while a **numeric** part runs the actual arithmetic. As long as the circuit's wiring is unchanged, the pattern is unchanged, so the symbolic result is computed once and cached. Manatee's backend does this KLU-style: `Analyze` inspects only the nonzero pattern, chooses a fill-reducing elimination order, and lays out the exact storage the factors will occupy; the pivot order is then frozen at the first numeric factorization, after which refactorize and solve are zero-allocation. This split is precisely what implements the change-cost tiers (design.md R4): tier 1 (RHS value changes) reuses the whole cached LU; tier 2 (conductance changes — switch toggles, Newton iterations) redoes numeric factorization against the reused symbolic plan; only tier 3 (topology changes) reruns `Analyze`. For a programmer: symbolic analysis is compile time, numeric factorization is run time — you recompile only when the source (wiring) changes, not when inputs change. The docs call this split the main numeric improvement over the sparky prototype, which refactored from scratch every time.

Standard sparse-matrix terminology; also called *symbolic analysis* or the *analyze phase* (KLU/UMFPACK/CHOLMOD).

**Where it appears:** `docs/design.md` R4 (change-cost tiers: "reused symbolic"); `docs/solver.md` Change-Cost Tiers table and implementation notes, and the Numerics backend plan (Analyze / frozen pivot order / zero-alloc refactorize).

**References:** T. A. Davis, *Direct Methods for Sparse Linear Systems*, SIAM (2006); T. A. Davis & E. Palamadai Natarajan (2010), "Algorithm 907: KLU, a direct sparse solver for circuit simulation problems," *ACM Trans. Math. Software* 37(3).

### symbolic pattern / frozen-refactor path
- Testing-strategy shorthand for two states of the sparse solver: the structural data the first factorization allocates (the *symbolic pattern*), and the steady state after it, where refactorizing allocates nothing (the *frozen-refactor path*).

The first call to `Factorize` on a new pattern must build the symbolic side — orderings, index arrays, factor storage — which necessarily allocates memory. Every later factorization of the same pattern reuses that storage with the pivot order frozen, touching only numbers: the frozen-refactor path, guaranteed zero-alloc. This matters operationally for the benchmark gates: a zero-allocation test that measures the *first* factorization would fail spuriously, so the gates warm up (run one throwaway `Factorize`) and then assert zero allocations on the second, which lands on the frozen path. EE analogy: the first power-up charges the reservoir capacitors — steady-state current draw is only meaningful after that inrush. The terms are informal project labels for the standard analyze-vs-refactor distinction, seen from the memory-allocation side.

**Project-coined phrasing**, closest standard concepts: *symbolic factorization* (the pattern) and the *refactorization* phase of a sparse direct solver (KLU's `klu_refactor`, which reuses both pattern and pivot order).

**Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` — Benchmarks / flake-isolation section (warm-up rule for the `ZeroAlloc` gate collection); the underlying mechanism is the solver backend described in `docs/solver.md` Numerics.

**References:** T. A. Davis & E. Palamadai Natarajan, "Algorithm 907: KLU, a direct sparse solver for circuit simulation problems," *ACM Trans. Math. Software* 37(3), 2010.

### Tolerance
- The maximum numeric difference two results may show and still count as "the same answer" when Manatee's output is compared against a reference.

Floating-point simulation never reproduces another simulator bit-for-bit, so every comparison in the test suite is a bounded diff rather than an equality check — the software analog of a measurement tolerance on a datasheet. The oracle tests compare manatee's node voltages and branch currents against ngspice within: DC, 0.1% relative error, or 1 µV absolute for values near zero (where relative error is meaningless); transient, a looser tolerance at matched timesteps, because Backward Euler and ngspice's default integrator legitimately differ — where exactness matters the tests force matching integration settings or compare only settled values. Lesson goldens carry their own per-expectation tolerance in machine-readable front-matter (probe, time, value, tolerance), so a lesson whose stated numbers drift out of tolerance fails CI. Note this is comparison tolerance, distinct from the EE sense of component tolerance (a resistor's ±5% manufacturing spread), though the two ideas rhyme.
**Standard term:** yes — in numerical software often split into relative tolerance (reltol) and absolute tolerance (abstol), the same pair of knobs SPICE itself uses for convergence.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests, Lesson Corpus as Goldens), `docs/curriculum.md` (Format — front-matter expectations).
- **References:** the ngspice manual (`.options reltol`/`abstol`); Higham, *Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms* (relative vs. absolute error); Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, UCB/ERL-M520.

### .tran / transient
A time-domain simulation: the circuit is stepped forward through time in small increments, producing waveforms (voltage and current as functions of time) rather than a single settled answer.

Where a DC operating point asks "where does this settle," transient analysis asks "what happens along the way" — capacitor charging curves, inductor current ramps, switch bounce. For a programmer, it is a discrete-time state machine: each timestep, the energy-storage components' differential behavior is approximated by algebraic "companion models" (Manatee uses Backward Euler per design.md R2), the resulting system is solved, and the state advances one step — conceptually like a physics engine's fixed-timestep integrator. `.tran` is the SPICE/ngspice card that requests this analysis. In our oracle tests, ngspice runs `.tran` on the same netlist and results are compared at matched timesteps with a looser tolerance than DC — Backward Euler and ngspice's default integrator legitimately differ, so tests either force matching integrator options or compare only settled values.

Standard term: transient analysis (time-domain analysis); `.tran` is standard SPICE syntax.

**Where it appears:** `docs/testing-strategy.md` (Oracle Tests, lesson corpus — the RC-transient lesson 04 exercises this path); `docs/design.md` R2 and Simulation Model.

**References:** Nagel (1975), *SPICE2: A Computer Program to Simulate Semiconductor Circuits*, memo UCB/ERL-M520; Pillage, Rohrer & Visweswariah, *Electronic Circuit and System Simulation Methods*; the ngspice manual (`.tran` analysis).

### transient timestep / dt
- The fixed time increment, in seconds, by which each transient step advances the simulated circuit — the `dt` in `Step(dt)`, and the value carried by the `maxTimeStep` field of a Falstad file's `$` header.

Smaller dt means finer time resolution at more solves per simulated second. Manatee deliberately holds dt *fixed* per profile (Stationeers 0.5 s, Vintage Story / tablet tick 0.05 s, with AC islands subdividing that into substeps) rather than adapting it as SPICE does, because the Backward Euler companion conductances G = C/dt depend on dt — constant dt means the matrix never changes for a linear island in steady operation, keeping every step in the cheap RHS-only tier. For an EE: dt is the sampling interval of the numerical integration; for a programmer: it is the frame duration of the simulation loop, and pinning it is a cache-friendliness decision (never invalidate the factorization). In the Falstad text format, `maxTimeStep` in the `$` header is the only header field with electrical meaning; our importer consumes it as the transient-dt hint and ignores the rest.

**Standard term:** yes — "timestep" / "step size", conventionally written *h* or *dt* in the numerical-methods literature; Falstad/circuitjs calls its header field `maxTimeStep`.

**Where it appears:** falstad-format.md §2 (`$` header) and §7 (importer contract); solver.md Change-Cost Tiers and Analyses; api.md §5 `SolverProfile`.

**References:** ngspice manual (`.tran tstep tstop`); Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design* (step-size and integration-method chapters).

### well-posed
- Describes a circuit (equivalently, its solver matrix) that has exactly one solution the numerics can actually find — no contradictions, no ambiguity.

An MNA solve turns the circuit into a system of linear equations; the system is well-posed when that matrix is non-singular, i.e. the equations pin down a unique answer. Ill-posed cases are physical contradictions or ambiguities: two ideal voltage sources with different values wired in parallel (contradictory equations — no solution), or a floating subcircuit with no ground reference (infinitely many solutions). In our docs the term appears in the Stationeers Legacy-Device Adaptor: every adapted generator carries a small internal series resistance precisely so that paralleled generators are well-posed — the resistance lets disagreeing sources split the difference through a real voltage drop instead of demanding the impossible. For a programmer: an ill-posed circuit is like a constraint system with either conflicting or under-determined constraints — the solver cannot return a value, only an error; adding series resistance is the schema fix that makes every input satisfiable. This usage matches the standard mathematical term (Hadamard's well-posedness: a solution exists, is unique, and depends continuously on the inputs); in circuit terms the common phrasing is that the MNA matrix is non-singular.
- **Where it appears:** `docs/stationeers.md` (Legacy-Device Adaptor); the failure-handling side (what happens when a circuit is *not* well-posed) is in `docs/solver.md`.
- **References:** Vlach & Singhal, *Computer Methods for Circuit Analysis and Design*, on singular MNA formulations; Ho, Ruehli & Brennan (1975), "The Modified Nodal Approach to Network Analysis", IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems.
