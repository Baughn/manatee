# Adversarial Design Review — Manatee (docs as of 2026-07-05)

Sources: `docs/design.md` (full), `docs/curriculum.md`, `docs/vintage-story.md`, plus `docs/solver.md`, `docs/stationeers.md`, `docs/compaction.md` for the specific mechanisms under attack. Each finding is tagged with the persona that found it (**[exploit]** min-maxer, **[newcomer]** frustrated newcomer) and ranked by severity.

---

## CRITICAL

### C1. [exploit+newcomer] SWER at the 12 V starter tier doesn't survive arithmetic
design.md declares SWER "the starter wiring idiom" (Grounding model) and 12 V DC "the starter tier" (Voltage standards), with per-electrode contact resistance at "tens of ohms." These three numbers collide. Scenario: a 12 V / 12 W lamp (12 Ω) behind two electrodes at 20 Ω each gets V_lamp = 12·12/52 ≈ 2.8 V — 5% of rated power. Even a generous 10 Ω total leaves the lamp at ~30% power. SWER is only viable when load resistance ≫ electrode resistance — i.e., at high voltage, which is why real SWER runs at 19.2 kV, and why curriculum lesson 14 teaches exactly this. At 12 V, SWER works only for milliamp loads (doorbell, telegraph — which is historically accurate ground-return use).
**Suggested answer:** pick one story. Either (a) scope the claim — SWER is the *telegraph/doorbell/fence* idiom and the mid-game *240 V distribution* idiom, and starter lighting is two-wire; or (b) keep SWER-first as deliberate discovery pedagogy ("why is my lamp dim?" → the electrode is the answer), in which case the electrode block's tooltip MUST attribute the loss ("9.2 V dropped across grounding rod — contact resistance 38 Ω") or the newcomer wall is undiagnosable in-fiction. As written, the doc reads as (a)-shaped claims with (b)-shaped consequences; the internal contradiction should be resolved in design.md before arc 1 is built.

### C2. [exploit] Stationeers legacy adaptor: across-tick lag is an energy pump
R18 / stationeers.md: legacy loads stamp G = P/V_prev²; legacy generators stamp as V-sources whose advertised-power current limit is "enforced by an across-tick clamp." Both lag one tick. Scenario: a logic chip (Stationeers has precise tick-rate automation) toggles a large load every power tick. On each rising tick, the generator's clamp reflects last tick's low draw, so the solve delivers I > P_available/V for the full 0.5 s tick — joules the vanilla generator never fueled. If vanilla accounting clamps debits at "available," the surplus is free energy into a battery; repeatable forever, fully automatable. The mirror case exists on loads (delivered P = P_wanted·(V/V_prev)² > P_wanted on voltage-rise ticks).
**Suggested answer:** per-adapted-device energy ledger, in the same across-tick style the adaptor already uses: deliverable energy this tick = advertised − accumulated debt; any over-delivery is debited from next tick's clamp. Cheap, allocation-free, and it converts the exploit into at-worst a one-tick loan that repays itself. Add "oscillating load vs adapted generator conserves energy over any window" to the adaptor-stability property test (testing-strategy.md already has the hook).

### C3. [newcomer] 5 Hz flicker is a photosensitivity hazard, not just a lesson
The 5 Hz base frequency with "visible lamp flicker included" is deliberately instructive (design.md, Frequency) — but 3–30 Hz rhythmic flashing is squarely the photosensitive-epilepsy trigger band, and the design has players living under it for the whole of arc 1–2 until high-pole-count alternators (an "advanced craft"). This is an accessibility ship-blocker independent of pedagogy.
**Suggested answer:** a client-side render option that smooths lamp luminance (sim untouched — the oscilloscope still shows the truth; only the light's rendered intensity is low-passed). Default value debatable; the option's existence is not. The lesson survives: flicker is still visible at default settings, the scope still teaches it, DC lighting remains the in-fiction fix.

## HIGH

### H1. [exploit] Earth is a free, fireproof, infinite-ampacity busbar
Earth is "an ordinary solver node" behind per-electrode contact resistance, and nothing in the limit system (R7, compaction.md limit envelopes) applies i²t or melting to the earth node or to electrodes. Scenario: at 240 V, a min-maxer never runs return conductors again — N parallel wet electrodes divide contact resistance arbitrarily ("better when wet" + a deliberate water pool = permanent bonus), and the earth path can never smoke, melt, ignite, or be chiseled by a griefer. Copper returns become strictly dominated at scale, deleting the wire-gauge economics the mod exists to teach.
**Suggested answer:** give electrodes their own limit envelope with real physics: overloaded grounding electrodes dry and glassify the surrounding soil, and resistance *rises* — genuine negative feedback, teachable, and it caps earth ampacity per electrode. Parallel-electrode farms then cost real material and land, which is honest engineering rather than an exploit. (Deliberate electrode wetting stays legal — that's real practice and a fine reward.)

### H2. [exploit] Island-boundary relaxation vs energy conservation — and the docs contradict themselves on exchange cadence
solver.md (Islands) says AC boundaries exchange amplitude+phase **per substep** with exponential smoothing; solver.md (Threading contract) says "coupling values exchange at tick boundaries only" for determinism. Both cannot hold once coupled islands run on different workers. Separately, the conservation guarantee is one-directional: "the coupling device clamps transfer to what its source island actually delivered" bounds the receiver, but under an oscillating secondary load both islands solve against smoothed, stale images of each other — correlated error that a storage element can rectify. Scenario: battery on each side of a decoupling transformer, square-wave load at a period near 1/α; any window where both sides' ledgers read positive integrates into SoC — a dupe that survives the per-exchange clamp because the clamp checks instants, not integrals.
**Suggested answer:** (1) resolve the cadence contradiction explicitly — likely "coupled islands are scheduled as one work unit and substep in lockstep," which preserves both the 1/20-cycle lag argument and determinism; (2) add a per-boundary running energy ledger: ∫P_out − ∫P_in − device losses must stay within tolerance, enforced two ways — as a CI invariant (extend the existing adaptor-stability property test) and at runtime by dumping any accumulated surplus into the transformer's heat output. Physically defensible (transformers do get hot) and it makes every conceivable leak manifest as heat, never as stored work.

### H3. [newcomer] The floating-system safety claim is a taught trap
design.md teaches "one wire of a floating system is safe to touch." Also design.md: bare conductors "ground when wet." Scenario: a player builds the advanced isolation setup by the book, rain wets one bare span somewhere out of sight, the system silently becomes grounded, and the "safe" wire kills them — they followed the game's own instruction and died to invisible state. This is realistic (it's why real isolated systems carry isolation monitors) and *does* trace to a mistake (unverified isolation), but the mistake is undiagnosable without a surface.
**Suggested answer:** the claim as taught must be "one wire of a *verified* floating system": (a) the multimeter reads wire-to-earth voltage, so the verification ritual exists from day one; (b) an isolation-monitor device (period-plausible: a lamp pair to earth — actual historical practice) as the advanced-build companion; (c) the curriculum hazard appendix teaches the check, not just the claim. Without this, the design's flagship safety pedagogy is a betrayal engine.

### H4. [newcomer/griefing] Live conductors hidden inside chiseled stone kill innocents
R17's dual occupancy deliberately buries cable inside decorative chiseled blocks (the oven is the showcase), and "chiseling into a live conductor shocks you" with lockout-tagout as the emergent skill. Scenario: a guest chisels what looks like plain decorative stone in a friend's base — no visible wire, no warning — and dies at 240 V. The design's own text ("if they told a friend to do it, the reason is presumably amusement") jokes about the malicious case but the innocent case is a real punishment of non-electricians for someone else's wiring choices.
**Suggested answer:** mostly already latent in R15 — the default-on hover tooltip shows V/I — but the docs must state explicitly that *chiseled blocks containing energized conductors surface this in block info*, not just dedicated cable blocks. Add a physical telegraph that survives the realism-purist config: energized AC conductors hum audibly at close range (diegetic, real, and teaches AC-vs-DC — DC's silence is itself a lesson the hazard appendix can name). First-contact damage should scale with voltage so interior 12 V control wiring stays a tingle, not a killing field.

## MEDIUM

### M1. [exploit] Mechanical↔electrical torque exchange can alias at 2f
Single-phase AC power pulsates at 2× electrical frequency: 10 Hz at the natural 5 Hz — exactly the mechanical network's ~100 ms (10 Hz) torque re-sum cadence. If the alternator's counter-torque is *sampled* rather than *averaged over the exact mechanical interval*, the 10 Hz ripple aliases to a DC torque offset of phase-dependent sign — free (or phantom) torque, i.e., a perpetual-motion seam for motor-spins-alternator builds. design.md says "time-averaged" but doesn't pin the averaging window.
**Suggested answer:** specify in vintage-story.md: counter-torque reported to `GetResistance()` is the mean over all electrical substeps since the last mechanical read, never an instantaneous sample. Add a standing invariant test: a closed motor→shaft→alternator→motor loop must monotonically decay from any initial condition (BE is dissipative electrically, so any test failure indicts the coupling). With that test in CI, the whole "spin your own alternator" exploit class is **acceptable because** both integrators are dissipative and the coupling is verified lossy.

### M2. [exploit/newcomer] Brownout dropout without hysteresis is a tick-rate flapper
R18: below threshold, load drops; above, it re-stamps at full G. Scenario: a network sized at ~100% capacity flaps every 0.5 s forever — doors cycling, machines resetting, a strobing base. Not free energy, but it's a self-inflicted denial-of-service the player can't read ("why is everything blinking?"), and a griefer can induce it by adding one marginal load to a shared grid.
**Suggested answer:** drop at V_low, rejoin at V_high (real undervoltage-relay practice), with per-device staggered rejoin delays (real load-shedding practice). Both are teachable mechanisms, not fudges. The voltage-collapse death spiral itself stays — it's correctly flagged as a shipped feature — but it should collapse *and stay collapsed* until the player sheds load, which is the legible version.

### M3. [newcomer] Ruins li-ion "sometimes already about to blow" violates the design's own no-random-hazards corollary
design.md's failure model: hazards "never [trace] to random external events." A looted battery that detonates on install is exactly that unless its state is inspectable beforehand. **Suggested answer:** pre-damaged cells are always diagnosable — visible swelling/scorch on the item model, anomalous voltage on the multimeter, warm to the touch (heat registry). The player's mistake becomes "installed salvage without checking it," which is diagnosable, in-fiction, and reinforces the instrument habit. One sentence in design.md closes the contradiction.

### M4. [griefing] Unclaimable long-distance infrastructure is the real griefing surface — turn fault-finding into gameplay
In-claim sabotage (rewiring, chisel attacks, plugging 240 V into a 12 V net) is covered by vanilla claim protection, since R17 rides the vanilla chisel path — **acceptable because** VS already adjudicates this. The delta vs vanilla is the mod *encourages* kilometer-scale unclaimed runs (distribution, telegraph) that one chisel stroke or one wet bare wire across the line can kill, and the victim gets no locate. **Suggested answer:** make fault-locating a taught skill: head-end breaker reports V/I at trip (V/I ratio ≈ distance-to-short — a real technique, one division), multimeter walks confirm. Griefers then create gameplay rather than pure loss. Consider whether energized traps *off-claim* need any cap beyond "12 V is harmless"; a hidden wet 240 V bare wire at a spawn trail is a kill vanilla cannot express — the hum telegraph from H4 is the passive counter.

### M5. [exploit] Salvage "at a loss" must be a mass loss, not a convenience loss
"Chiseling out cable material salvages it at a loss (wire bits, not intact wire)." If wire bits re-smelt to full ingot mass, the loss is only crafting time and the degradation economics have a free loop (strip, re-smelt, re-draw). Also, sustained overload *deleting* insulation voxels is free demolition labor for repurposing cable as bare fence wire. **Suggested answer:** one line in design.md — salvage and burn-off both destroy a fraction of metal/resin mass. Small, but economies are min-maxed at the margins.

## Frustration walk — arc 1, tablet never opened

The biggest structural gap first: **the vanilla VS survival handbook is mentioned nowhere in any doc.** It is the game's native "explain in-fiction" surface and the only channel that reaches a player who ignores the tablet. Every wall below needs a handbook entry as its floor; the tablet is the ceiling. Recommend adding handbook authorship to R15/R16's scope explicitly.

## The educational thesis — where realism becomes tedium

1. **Missing meta-rule: every mastered skill needs an automation off-ramp.** The design has the right instinct twice (pre-wired elevator controller; DC lighting vs flicker) but doesn't state the principle. Generator synchronization is the acute case: a genuine thrill the first five times, then a chore every restart, forever. A craftable **sync-check breaker** (blocks out-of-phase closure; player still matches speed roughly) preserves the skill while amortizing the tedium — same shape as the controller block. Recommend promoting "learned it → automate it" to a design principle in design.md; it is the difference between a curriculum and a treadmill.
2. **Maintenance friction is unregulated.** R13 makes *placement* frictionless but nothing covers *replacement*: zinc plates, carbon rods (arc lamps), sun lamps, burned cable sections. Realism says these consume; tedium is decided entirely by interaction cost (one-click swap vs chisel-out-rebuild) and interval tuning. Extend R13's frictionless mandate to maintenance explicitly, or arc 2 becomes a lamp-changing simulator.
3. **Weather-coupled electrics create intermittent mystery faults** — dry-season SWER brownouts, rain-shorted bare spans. Intermittent failure is the least-fun failure class (the user's SRE framing: flaky infra). It's honest physics, so keep it, but it raises the bar on M-class telegraphs: wetness/contact-R must be *readable* (electrode tooltip), or "it worked yesterday" becomes the mod's reputation.
4. **The voltage-collapse death spiral (R18) is pedagogy on the tablet and pure frustration on a live base** — with M2's hysteresis it degrades legibly, without it it's a strobe. Same physics, one design choice apart.
5. **Acceptable-because ledger:** 5 Hz-until-pole-count progression (real reward, real path, DC escape hatch — keep, modulo C3); consumable-zinc early game (teaches storage's raison d'être — keep, with #2's swap ergonomics and a viable waterwheel-only path); chisel-vulnerable live cables (LOTO is genuinely the right lesson — keep, modulo H4's innocent-protection surfaces); manual relay logic difficulty (controller block already answers it); earth-return physics complexity (it's the mod's best idea — keep, modulo C1's arithmetic and H1's limits).

## Summary for the orchestrator

Two findings need design-doc changes before code: **C1** (SWER/12 V numbers contradict — pick the story) and **H2's cadence contradiction** (per-substep vs per-tick boundary exchange in solver.md). Two need mechanism additions with tests: **C2** (adaptor energy ledger + oscillation property test) and **H2** (boundary energy ledger, surplus→heat). One is an unconditional ship gate: **C3** (flicker accessibility option). The rest are one-line doc amendments (M3, M5), telegraph/tooltip requirements that mostly ride existing R15 infrastructure (H3, H4, frustration table), and one new authoring workstream the docs currently omit entirely (vanilla handbook entries). The griefing story is largely **acceptable because** vanilla claims adjudicate in-claim sabotage; the genuine delta is unclaimed long-line vulnerability (M4), best answered by making fault-locating a taught skill rather than by protection.
